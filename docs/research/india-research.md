# India — Asbestos Research Profile

**Researcher:** Claude (ToxinFree)
**Date:** 2026-04-07
**Status:** Draft — Pending human review
**Confidence:** HIGH — India's asbestos situation is extensively documented through peer-reviewed epidemiology, Supreme Court records, parliamentary debates, investigative journalism (ICIJ, Al Jazeera, Scroll), and activist archives (BANI, ICIJ, Mongabay). The factual picture is clear: India is the world's largest asbestos importer, has blocked international chrysotile listing for years, and has no national ban on chrysotile import or use. Key gaps: no reliable national mesothelioma mortality figure (documented massive undercount); no comprehensive map of asbestos-containing buildings; occupational compensation data is near-zero despite documented large-scale worker harm.

## 1. Regulatory Timeline

India's asbestos history spans nearly a century — from the British colonial introduction of the industry in 1934, through decades of expanding domestic manufacturing and mining, to the 1993 effective end of domestic asbestos mining and the present status as the world's largest asbestos importer. Throughout this period, India's regulatory trajectory has been the opposite of the global trend: while over 70 countries banned asbestos, India's consumption grew, and the government actively defended chrysotile use in international forums.

### Context

India's regulatory trajectory represents one of the most consequential divergences from global asbestos consensus in any major economy. While the domestic asbestos mining ban of 1993 was presented internationally as evidence of regulatory responsiveness, it was paired with the active expansion of chrysotile *imports* — which grew from 99,000 tonnes in 2000 to 485,182 tonnes in 2023, a nearly fivefold increase. The industry's lobbying infrastructure — centered on the Asbestos Cement Products Manufacturers' Association (ACPMA), the Asbestos Information Centre in New Delhi, and coordination with the International Chrysotile Association — has consistently presented chrysotile as "safe" under controlled use conditions, a position rejected by WHO, ILO, IARC, and the Collegium Ramazzini.

The poorest Indians bear a disproportionate share of the exposure burden. Asbestos-cement corrugated roofing is the dominant low-cost roofing material in Indian slums, informal settlements, and rural housing — an estimated 79% of India's Dalit population (approximately 200 million people) live in homes with asbestos roofs. The asbestos industry markets its products specifically to the rural and urban poor as an affordable, durable roofing solution — using the poverty of potential victims as a commercial argument for continued use.

## 2. Historias de Resistencia (Activism Stories)

### Individual Stories

#### Gopal Krishna — Ban Asbestos Network of India (BANI)

- **Who:** Environmental lawyer and co-founder of the Ban Asbestos Network of India (BANI) and founder of the Toxics Watch Alliance (TWA). Based in New Delhi.
- **The catalyst:** As a young activist in the early 2000s, Krishna began investigating India's regulatory treatment of asbestos. In 2008, he used the Right to Information Act to demand documents on asbestos dangers from government ministries — the first time official records on asbestos health hazards were made public in India. The documents revealed that the government had possessed information about asbestos carcinogenicity that it had not acted on.
- **What they did:** Founded BANI in 2000 in coalition with workers' movements, trade unions, public health groups, human rights organizations, and researcher-activists. Filed a complaint with India's National Human Rights Commission (NHRC) in June 2011. Engaged the National Green Tribunal on asbestos issues. Built sustained international visibility through IBAS and the global ban-asbestos network. Launched the Asbestos Free India campaign. In 2024, raised the case of the Indian Government purchasing an asbestos-contaminated property in Washington D.C. — a case that generated significant media coverage.
- **Impact:** BANI is the primary civil society force in India documenting asbestos victims, challenging government "safe use" studies, and maintaining public pressure for a ban. The 2025 Education Ministry school guideline — while limited in scope — represents the first formal government acknowledgment that asbestos in schools is unacceptable, and BANI explicitly welcomed it as a step toward a comprehensive ban. The 22-year campaign (2002–2024) has kept India's asbestos crisis visible despite sustained industry opposition.

#### Roro Village Tribal Communities — Jharkhand

- **Who:** Tribal communities (Adivasi populations) in four villages surrounding the abandoned Roro Hills asbestos mine in West Singhbhum district, Jharkhand: Roro Village and adjacent settlements. The communities include families of former mine workers (many of whom worked the mine between 1963 and 1983) and people who have lived with asbestos soil contamination since the mine's abandonment.
- **The catalyst:** After the Roro Hills mine closed in 1983, Hyderabad Industries abandoned the site without remediation. Asbestos waste piles — some meters high — were left on the hilltops above the villages. Contaminated soil, waterways, and the mine site itself remained. A preliminary health survey documented elevated rates of lung disease, shortness of breath, and hemoptysis in the surrounding villages.
- **What they did:** Community members and local advocates have pursued remediation through the courts. A case was filed before the National Green Tribunal in 2019, resulting in an order directing the Jharkhand government to undertake scientific removal. Separately, a community claim was brought before the NGT in 2014 regarding Hyderabad Industries Limited's contamination and demanding cleanup and compensation.
- **Impact:** Limited. As of 2022, the waste piles remain, the state government's 2019 commitment to cleanup has not been implemented, and compensation for affected workers has not been paid. The communities continue to live with soil containing up to 14.3% asbestos fiber. The case represents a pattern documented across post-colonial asbestos mining regions: industrial departure without remediation, leaving communities with legacy contamination and no viable legal recourse.

#### Former Kymore Factory Workers — Turner Newall / Everest Industries Trust Fund

- **Who:** Workers and community members affected by the Turner Newall factory in Kymore, Madhya Pradesh — India's first asbestos factory (established 1934). After Turner Newall (UK) became insolvent in 2001, a trust fund was established for compensation of asbestos disease victims.
- **The catalyst:** Decades of asbestos-cement manufacturing at Kymore, combined with the documented dumping of asbestos waste on surrounding land through the 1990s, created a large population of affected workers and community members. The bankruptcy of Turner Newall in 2001 threatened to extinguish compensation claims but instead led to establishment of a compensation trust.
- **Impact:** Since medical camps began in 2013 at Kymore, over 400 people have been diagnosed with asbestos-related diseases and received compensation from the Turner Newall Trust Fund. The contaminated land — estimated at 600,000 square metres at a cleanup cost of up to $88 million — remains a legacy contamination site. The Kymore case is one of the few instances in India where former asbestos industry workers received any compensation at all.

## 4. Exposure Sources ("Where Was It?")

### Common Asbestos-Containing Materials in India

India has been simultaneously a historic producer, an importer, and a manufacturer of asbestos products throughout the twentieth and twenty-first centuries. The material is embedded in the housing stock, public infrastructure, industrial buildings, and transportation infrastructure of the country.

- **Asbestos-cement corrugated roofing sheets:** The single most common asbestos-containing product in India, and the primary vehicle of ongoing exposure. Asbestos-cement roofing is used across India's rural and peri-urban housing stock — specifically marketed to low-income households as affordable, durable roofing. Leading brands include Charminar (Hyderabad Industries), Everest (Everest Industries), and other regional manufacturers. Approximately 79% of Dalit households — the most economically marginalized caste communities — live under asbestos roofs.
- **Asbestos-cement flat sheets (partitions, ceilings, wall cladding):** Used in industrial buildings, schools, government offices, hospitals, and commercial premises from the 1950s through the present. The Education Ministry's 2025 ban on asbestos in KVs and JNVs directly addresses legacy sheets in school buildings.
- **Asbestos-cement water pipes:** Used in water distribution infrastructure, rural water supply schemes, and sewerage across India from the 1940s through the late 1990s.
- **Friction materials (brake linings, clutch pads):** India's automotive industry — one of the world's largest — has been a major consumer of asbestos friction materials. As of the most recent data available, chrysotile-containing brake linings and clutch pads are still used in India's vehicle maintenance sector.
- **Gaskets and packing:** Industrial gaskets in chemical plants, power stations, and manufacturing facilities.
- **Asbestos textiles:** Heat-resistant gloves, blankets, and protective clothing in industrial settings.
- **Naturally occurring asbestos in mining waste:** The abandoned Roro Hills mine (Jharkhand) and other former mining sites contaminate surrounding soils. Communities living near abandoned mines are exposed through soil contact, windblown dust, and contaminated water.

### Key Locations of Exposure

- **Kymore, Madhya Pradesh:** Site of India's first asbestos factory (1934, Turner Newall). Asbestos waste dumped over 600,000 square metres of surrounding land through the 1990s. Currently Everest Industries factory site. Medical camps since 2013 have diagnosed 400+ workers with asbestos-related diseases.
- **Roro Hills, West Singhbhum, Jharkhand:** Abandoned chrysotile mine (1963–1983, Hyderabad Industries). Asbestos contamination up to 14.3% of residential soil. Tribal communities continue to live within the contamination zone. NGT remediation order (2019) remains unimplemented as of 2022.
- **Rajasthan (Bhilwara, Ajmer districts):** Historically the center of Indian asbestos mining. Multiple former mine sites, some with illegal post-ban operations documented. Indian Bureau of Mines has conducted pollution surveys of Rajasthan asbestos processing plants.
- **Hyderabad (Telangana) / Andhra Pradesh:** Headquarters of Hyderabad Industries Limited (Charminar brand); multiple asbestos-cement manufacturing facilities in Andhra Pradesh and surrounding states.
- **Alang, Bhavnagar, Gujarat — ship recycling yards:** The Alang-Sosiya ship recycling complex is the world's largest, handling 47% of global ship scrapping along approximately 10 km of coastline. Approximately 60,000 workers are employed, many of them migrant workers from Bihar, Orissa, and other states. Ships built before the global asbestos phase-out contain substantial asbestos insulation, pipe lagging, and gaskets. Studies have documented that 16% of chest X-rays in an asbestos-handler cohort showed linear shadows, and 39% showed restrictive pulmonary impairment.
- **Mumbai / Chennai / Kolkata — industrial and textile factories:** Major industrial cities with historic exposure from asbestos-cement, textile, and shipbuilding industries. A prevalence study at a Mumbai asbestos composite mill found asbestos-related disorders in a documented worker cohort.
- **Urban housing stock (nationwide):** Asbestos-cement roofing on tens of millions of low-income urban and peri-urban homes. Rural India: asbestos roofing is the dominant building material in tribal and Dalit communities.

## 5. Corporate Responsibility

### Hyderabad Industries Limited (formerly Hyderabad Asbestos Cement Products Ltd.)

- **Role:** Asbestos mining (Roro Hills, 1963–1983) and asbestos-cement manufacturing (Charminar brand corrugated and flat sheets). India's leading asbestos-cement producer.
- **Period of activity:** Mining: 1963–1983. Manufacturing: ongoing (currently primarily fiber-cement products, but historically asbestos-cement).
- **Legal outcomes:** In 2014, a community living near Roro Hills filed a case before the National Green Tribunal claiming the company's abandoned mine caused health damage and demanding cleanup and compensation. The case is documented as ongoing. The company denied liability. [Business & Human Rights Resource Centre — Hyderabad Industries lawsuit](https://www.business-humanrights.org/en/latest-news/hyderabad-industries-lawsuit-re-asbestos-contamination-india/)
- **Current status:** Publicly listed company on Indian stock exchanges. Has transitioned some production lines to non-asbestos fiber-cement products, but legacy contamination at Roro Hills remains unaddressed.
- **Source:** [Mongabay India — Jharkhand tribal village (2022)](https://india.mongabay.com/2022/04/the-ghost-of-asbestos-mining-continues-to-haunt-this-tribal-village-in-jharkhand/); [PubMed — Blighted Hills of Roro (2003)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/12967162/)

### Turner and Newall PLC / Everest Industries Limited (Kymore)

- **Role:** India's first asbestos factory operator (Kymore, 1934). Operated three factories in Kymore, Mulund (Bombay), and Calcutta. Produced asbestos-cement corrugated sheets and other asbestos products for the Indian market.
- **Period of activity:** 1934 through insolvency in 2001 under Turner Newall. The Kymore factory subsequently operated under the Everest Industries brand.
- **Legal outcomes:** Following Turner Newall's UK insolvency in 2001, a compensation trust fund was established. Medical camps at Kymore beginning in 2013 have identified 400+ victims who received compensation from the trust. The contaminated land (600,000 sq m, cleanup cost estimated at $88m) remains unaddressed.
- **Significance:** The Kymore case is the most documented instance in India of corporate asbestos liability being partially acknowledged and compensated. It resulted from a UK insolvency process, not from Indian courts or regulatory enforcement.
- **Source:** [IBAS — Legacy of European Asbestos Industry in India](https://ibasecretariat.org/km-legacy-of-european-asbestos-industry-continues-in-india.php); [Doughty Street Chambers](https://www.doughtystreet.co.uk/news/environmental-exposure-asbestos-kills-indian-people)

### Visaka Industries Limited

- **Role:** One of India's largest asbestos-cement manufacturers, with factories in Andhra Pradesh, Tamil Nadu, West Bengal, and Karnataka. Produces asbestos-cement roofing sheets under the "Visaka" brand. New plant in Tumkur, Karnataka opened in April 2005.
- **Industry position:** In a Toronto Star interview, a Visaka Industries executive stated the company's asbestos cement products were "not at all hazardous to workers or consumers" — a position in direct contradiction to WHO, IARC, and ILO guidance.
- **Source:** [Galiher Law — India's Growing Asbestos Industry (referencing Toronto Star)](https://galiherlaw.com/indias-growing-asbestos-industry-takes-a-heavy-toll-on-workers/)

### Asbestos Cement Products Manufacturers' Association (ACPMA)

- **Role:** Industry lobbying association representing India's asbestos-cement manufacturers. Active in government engagement to defend chrysotile use, in promoting "controlled use" narrative, and in blocking regulatory action.
- **Key documented action:** ACPMA co-sponsored a 2012 government-commissioned safety study on chrysotile, creating a conflict of interest in what was presented as an independent scientific assessment.
- **International activity:** Coordinates with International Chrysotile Association (ICA) and maintains the Asbestos Information Centre in New Delhi.
- **Source:** [PubMed — Analysis of Indian Government position on asbestos health effects (2023)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37359063/)

### Context

India's mesothelioma data illustrates a systemic crisis of documentation. The 20:1 ratio between hospital-identified mesothelioma cases and official cancer registry cases for the 2012–2016 period is not primarily a medical phenomenon — it reflects structural failures: only 16% of the population is covered by cancer registries, most pathologists are not trained to diagnose mesothelioma, occupational history-taking is rare, cases are frequently misdiagnosed as tuberculosis (a common respiratory condition in India, which shares symptoms with asbestosis), and there is no financial incentive for diagnosis — because there is virtually no compensation pathway for workers.

The 4,513 projected mesothelioma deaths from the Alang ship recycling cohort alone — workers employed between 1994 and 2002 — represent a specific, bounded population for whom the toll is approximately quantifiable. This figure does not include the millions of workers in asbestos-cement factories, construction, vehicle maintenance, or residents under asbestos roofs.

The 20-year projection of 600,000 asbestos-related cancer cases assumes continued use at current rates. As India's asbestos imports have grown every year since 2000, this projection is likely conservative.

## 7. Current Status

- **Chrysotile imports continue at record levels:** India imported 485,182 tonnes of chrysotile in 2023 — the highest recorded figure, representing 56% of all global asbestos imports. Sources are Russia, Brazil, and Kazakhstan. No restriction on chrysotile imports exists.
- **Manufacturing ongoing:** India's asbestos-cement industry — roofing sheets, flat sheets, and pipes — continues operation at scale. Key manufacturers include Hyderabad Industries, Visaka Industries, Everest Industries, and regional producers.
- **Government position (2022):** Ministry of Environment, Forests and Climate Change confirmed no proposal to ban asbestos in India. The government continues to support "controlled use" of chrysotile.
- **Education Ministry school ban (2025):** The first formal sector-specific restriction: asbestos banned in new construction and refurbishment of KVs and JNVs. A significant symbolic step but covers only centrally-funded government schools, not the broader school system or other sectors.
- **NGT judgment on schools (October 2025):** Directed the Union Government to review scientific evidence before taking a policy decision on asbestos use in all schools. This is a significant judicial signal but not yet a binding ban.
- **Rotterdam Convention COP-12 (May 2025):** Chrysotile asbestos listing in Annex III remains pending. India continues to be among the countries blocking consensus.
- **Roro Hills remediation (2022 status):** NGT remediation order (2019) remains unimplemented. Tribal communities continue to live with asbestos soil contamination. No compensation paid to affected workers or communities.
- **Alang ship recycling:** Workers continue to be exposed to asbestos during ship dismantling. While safety protocols have improved, enforcement remains inconsistent and the projected mesothelioma burden from past exposures is unavoidable.
- **Illegal mining:** Asbestos mining continues illegally in Jharkhand, Rajasthan, and Andhra Pradesh at small scale, with leases expired and companies mining without authorization.
- **2025 schools initiative:** Minister Jayant Singh Chaudhary has described asbestos ban as "a health imperative, not just a moral issue" — representing the strongest language from a sitting minister and suggesting possible future broader policy action. [BANI — Education Ministry takes step (June 2025)](https://www.asbestosfreeindia.org/2025/06/education-ministry-takes-step-for.html)

## Verification Notes

1. **Mesothelioma mortality figure**: No reliable national mesothelioma deaths-per-year figure exists for India. The figure often cited in advocacy ("30,000 deaths/year") is an estimate that appears in some sources but is not documented in peer-reviewed literature. The best documented figures are: 2,213 mesothelioma cases from 83 hospitals (2012–2022), with a documented 20:1 undercount vs. official registries. **The ROADMAP's estimate of "~30,000 deaths/year" should not be presented as a verified figure** — it is advocacy-derived and exceeds what the available data supports.
2. **ban_status correction**: The current countries.json entry shows `ban_status: "no_ban"`. This is technically arguable — chrysotile imports are unrestricted — but the restriction on asbestos *mining* since 1993, and the ban on asbestos waste in ships (2011), make `partial_ban` the more accurate status per the SKILL's classification guide ("Any asbestos restriction legally enacted → not all forms → partial_ban").
3. **Gopal Krishna direct quote**: No verbatim quote was identified in public-domain English-language sources. The ToxicsWatch and BANI blogs are in English but primarily publish analytical commentary rather than interviews. A direct interview would be needed.
4. **Roro Hills cleanup status**: All sources citing 2022 confirm the NGT order remains unimplemented. 2024 or 2025 status should be verified by a human researcher via direct contact with NGT or Jharkhand state authorities.
5. **"79% of Dalits under asbestos roofs"**: This specific statistic appears in multiple advocacy sources but its primary research basis is not clearly identified in the sources reviewed. It should be traced to its original study before being presented as a primary statistic.
