## 1. Regulatory Timeline

Turkey was a producer and consumer of asbestos for the majority of the twentieth century, with naturally occurring chrysotile and tremolite deposits spread across Anatolia. The country consumed an estimated 1.2 million tonnes of asbestos between 1900 and 2003. Regulatory action came gradually, beginning with a crocidolite import ban in 1993 and culminating in a complete production and use ban effective December 31, 2010 — aligned with Turkey's EU accession candidacy obligations under Directive 1999/77/EC.

### Context

Turkey's path to the 2010 ban reflects both the challenges of a country with abundant naturally occurring asbestos deposits and the leverage of EU accession candidacy as a driver of regulatory convergence. Unlike most European countries where the ban was driven primarily by occupational health concerns, Turkey faced a simultaneous and in some ways more dramatic challenge: mesothelioma clusters caused not only by industrial use but by geological exposure — particularly in central Anatolia, where villagers lived in, built with, and coated their homes using asbestos-bearing soils and stones.

The famous Cappadocia mesothelioma epidemic is, in the scientific literature, attributed primarily to erionite — a fibrous zeolite mineral rather than asbestos — though both minerals co-occur in Anatolian geology. This distinction matters epidemiologically: erionite may be 200 to 800 times more carcinogenic per fiber than chrysotile asbestos, and the Cappadocia data showed family-based genetic susceptibility patterns unique to erionite exposure. Yet the broader Anatolian public health crisis includes genuine asbestos exposure from both industrial use and naturally occurring chrysotile and tremolite deposits in soils used as building and insulating materials.

The 2023 earthquakes dramatically worsened Turkey's asbestos legacy: over a century of installed asbestos-containing materials were pulverized across ten provinces, creating hazardous demolition dust conditions that persisted for more than a year in some areas, particularly in Hatay province.

## 2. Historias de Resistencia (Activism Stories)

### Individual Stories

#### Prof. Dr. Izzettin (Y.I.) Baris — Discoverer of the Cappadocia Epidemic

- **Who:** Pulmonary physician at Hacettepe University, Ankara. Widely recognized as the discoverer of the Cappadocia mesothelioma epidemic and one of the most important figures in the global history of asbestos-related disease research.
- **The catalyst:** In October 1975, Baris and his team traveled to Karain village in Cappadocia after receiving unusual reports of respiratory disease deaths. They diagnosed 12 mesothelioma patients within a single visit — an incidence roughly 1,000 times higher than comparable central Anatolian villages. At a time when mesothelioma was considered almost exclusively an occupational disease of asbestos workers, finding it in a rural farming village with no industrial exposure was medically unprecedented.
- **What they did:** Baris published the first findings in the journal *Thorax* in 1978 alongside colleagues, drawing international scientific attention. Over the subsequent two decades, he established a dedicated clinic for mesothelioma patients in Tuzkoy village and led a prospective 23-year study of 891 villagers aged 20 and older, tracking the natural history of the epidemic. Results published in the *Journal of the National Cancer Institute* (2006) showed 372 deaths among followed subjects, of which 119 (32%) were mesothelioma — confirming the extraordinary mortality concentration. The epidemiological work done by Baris and subsequent researchers (including Drs. Umran and Meral Dogan) ultimately changed the global scientific understanding of mineral fiber carcinogenesis.
- **Impact:** Baris's findings triggered international scientific investigation of erionite as a carcinogen and directly informed the decision to relocate the affected villages. His research also influenced IARC's classification of erionite as a Group 1 carcinogen. The clinic he established in Tuzkoy provided palliative care to mesothelioma patients in a community otherwise without medical support.

#### Prof. Dr. Muzaffer Metintaş — Turkish Mesothelioma Working Group

- **Who:** Chest physician and researcher at Eskişehir Osmangazi University; founder of the Applied Research Centre for Lung and Pleural Diseases (APKAM) and leader of the Turkish Mesothelioma Working Group.
- **The catalyst:** By the late 2000s, Turkey had no national mesothelioma registry, no systematic environmental asbestos survey, and no data on the scale of occupational versus environmental exposure burden. With the 2010 ban approaching and uncertainty about the true scope of the country's asbestos crisis, Metintaş recognized that clinical management of mesothelioma patients and public health policy were both operating blind.
- **What they did:** Metintaş established APKAM at Eskişehir Osmangazi University in 2012 — Turkey's leading mesothelioma research and clinical center. He organized the Turkish Mesothelioma Working Group, which conducted the national TUNMES-EAECP surveillance program (Turkish Mesothelioma Surveillance and Environmental Asbestos Exposure Control Program). The program conducted a formal survey in 2013, identifying 5,617 confirmed mesothelioma cases across Turkey, measuring standardized incidence rates, and identifying 379 villages with continuing environmental asbestos exposure affecting an estimated 158,068 people in high-risk rural areas.
- **Impact:** Created Turkey's first systematic national mesothelioma data infrastructure. The program identified that 66.5% of cases had rural (environmental/geological) exposure and projected 2,511 additional mesothelioma cases in exposed populations between 2013 and 2033. This data has underpinned subsequent Turkish public health policy on village relocation and occupational health guidance.

### Civil Society & Research Advocacy

#### Turkish Thoracic Society — Mesothelioma Committee

- **Who:** The Turkish Thoracic Society (Türk Toraks Derneği), the national medical society for respiratory medicine, maintains a dedicated Mesothelioma Committee that coordinates clinical standards and research.
- **Activity:** The committee has published clinical guidelines for mesothelioma management, coordinated the multicentric clinical studies that produced the national survival data (282 patients, 11 medical oncology centers), and raised physician awareness of occupational asbestos exposure patterns — particularly important given evidence that occupational exposure is systematically underrecognized compared to environmental exposure.
- **Key finding from their research:** A 2018 paper documented that at least 1 in 5 male mesothelioma patients in Istanbul had identifiable occupational asbestos exposure (shipbuilding: 17%, brake/clutch manufacturing: 16%) that was frequently not recognized or documented in clinical settings due to low physician awareness and absence of formal occupational history-taking protocols.
- **Sources:** [American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine — Occupational asbestos exposure in Istanbul (2018)](https://www.atsjournals.org/doi/full/10.1164/rccm.201810-1922LE); [ERS — Mesothelioma abstract (2017)](https://erj.ersjournals.com/content/50/suppl_61/PA413)

#### NGO Advocacy on Demolition and Earthquake Debris

- **Context:** Turkey lacks a dedicated asbestos-specific civil society organization equivalent to SOS Amianto in Portugal or the Asbestos Victims Support Groups Forum in the UK. However, a coalition of environmental NGOs, investigative journalists, and union organizers have increasingly raised public asbestos concerns — particularly around urban demolition and, after 2023, earthquake debris removal.
- **Activities documented:** NGOs and local political leaders issued warnings about unregulated demolition of asbestos-containing buildings in Turkish cities — particularly as urban transformation projects accelerated from the 2010s onward. After the February 2023 earthquakes, international NGOs and Turkish civil society organizations raised formal alarms about asbestos in the 116–200 million tonnes of earthquake rubble, and investigative journalists published reports (via GIJN — the Global Investigative Journalism Network) on inadequate worker protection in debris removal.
- **Specific concern:** Demolition contractors and building owners frequently failed to conduct pre-demolition asbestos surveys, even in districts where these were legally required. Authorities were documented as frequently ignoring alarms raised by civil society organizations. In Hatay province, over a year after the February 2023 earthquakes, demolition sites near residential areas remained shrouded in dust with inadequate suppression measures.
- **Sources:** [Duvar English — Asbestos threatens public health in Turkey (2019)](https://www.duvarenglish.com/health-2/2019/12/30/asbestos-continues-to-threaten-public-health-in-turkey); [GIJN — Asbestos dangers in Turkey's earthquake zone](https://gijn.org/stories/asbestos-dangers-lurking-turkeys-earthquake-zone/); [PMC — Post-earthquake asbestos exposure study 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11181204/)

## 4. Exposure Sources ("Where Was It?")

### Common Asbestos-Containing Materials in Turkey

Turkey was simultaneously a consumer of imported asbestos and a country with significant domestic asbestos deposits. The primary form in industrial use was chrysotile; tremolite and other amphiboles occurred naturally across Anatolian geology. An estimated 1.2 million tonnes of asbestos were consumed in Turkish industry between 1900 and 2003, primarily in asbestos-cement construction products.

- **Asbestos-cement corrugated roofing sheets:** The dominant product by volume. Used in residential, agricultural, industrial, and school construction throughout Turkey during the peak usage era. Post-ban, fiber-cement alternatives (e.g., Atermit Turkey products) replaced asbestos-cement.
- **Asbestos-cement flat sheets and cladding:** Interior and exterior panels, facades, soffit boards. Widely installed in apartment buildings from the 1960s onward.
- **Asbestos-cement water pipes:** Water distribution and sewage infrastructure.
- **Pipe insulation and boiler lagging:** Used extensively in shipyards (particularly in Tuzla and İzmit), industrial plants, and naval vessels. Shipbuilding workers faced high-risk amphibole asbestos exposure.
- **Friction materials (brake linings and clutch pads):** The last industrial sector to receive a chrysotile phase-out grace period under the 2005 partial ban. Brake and clutch manufacturing was identified as the second highest occupational risk sector (16%) in Turkish mesothelioma studies.
- **Asbestos textiles:** Gloves, protective clothing, and cloth for industrial applications.
- **Coating materials, tiling adhesives, plasters:** Used in residential and commercial construction.
- **Acoustic ceiling systems:** Installed in offices, schools, and commercial buildings through the 1980s.
- **Naturally occurring asbestos in building materials ("white soil"):** Villagers across central Anatolia — particularly in the regions of Eskişehir, Kütahya, Bilecik, Yozgat, Sivas, and Diyarbakır — used locally quarried asbestos-bearing soil as whitewash, roof insulation/waterproofing, floor surfacing, and even as a baby powder substitute. Sweeping and resurfacing activities aerosolized asbestos fibers inside homes. This pathway of exposure — entirely separate from industrial use — is estimated to affect up to 5–10 million villagers in high-risk areas and 158,068 people in formally identified high-risk rural areas. As of 2024, some village populations continue this practice.

### Key Locations of Exposure

- **Eskişehir region (western Turkey):** Both industrial asbestos processing and geological chrysotile/tremolite deposits. Chrysotile and tremolite documented in Palaeozoic-Mesozoic metamorphic and Triassic ophiolitic rock units. The Eskişehir region has been a focus of mineral fiber health research and is among the highest-incidence provinces for mesothelioma in Turkey. [Clays and Clay Minerals — Eskişehir fibrous tremolite/chrysotile study](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/clay-minerals/article/abs/characterization-and-distribution-of-fibrous-tremolite-and-chrysotile-minerals-in-the-eskisehir-region-of-western-turkey/43E2332F56939AFAF4082E9FD6F194E3)

- **Karain, Tuzkoy (Tuzköy), and Sarihidir villages, Cappadocia (Nevşehir province, central Turkey):** Sites of the historically unprecedented mesothelioma epidemic. The causative agent is primarily erionite — a fibrous zeolite mineral found in local volcanic tuff used as building material — not asbestos, though both fibers occur in the local geology. Approximately 50% of all deaths in these villages (including non-disease deaths) were caused by mesothelioma. All three villages have been subject to state-sponsored relocation programs since 2001. [PMC — Cappadocia mesothelioma review (2017)](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5497117/)

- **Diyarbakır province (southeastern Turkey):** Tremolite and chrysotile deposits in multiple villages. Documented ambient fiber concentrations of 4.9 fibers/cm³ at mine sites and 1.24 fibers/cm³ in house plaster — both elevated. Environmentally exposed residents showed geometric mean asbestos body counts of 5.20 AB/ml. One of the highest-incidence birth provinces for mesothelioma in the national surveillance. [PMC — Diyarbakır asbestos exposure](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4771638/)

- **Beypınari, Sivas province (central Turkey):** Chrysotile deposits in Central Anatolian Ophiolites approximately 90 km southeast of Sivas. Villagers continued using asbestos-bearing soil for home insulation after the 2010 ban. [Springer — Journal of the Geological Society of India (2016)](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12594-016-0388-7)

- **Çankırı and Ankara regions:** Numerous occurrences of fibrous chrysotile and tremolite in Cretaceous dunite-harzburgite and pyroxenite thrust fault zones. Fibers occur in mesh, suboriented bundle, and vein-filling textures — forms with demonstrated carcinogenic potential. [Clays and Clay Minerals — Çankırı/Ankara chrysotile/tremolite](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1346/CCMN.2018.064088)

- **Tuzla and İzmit shipyards (Marmara Sea coast):** Major centers of Turkish shipbuilding and repair. Shipyard workers identified as the highest-risk occupational group for asbestos exposure in Turkey (17% of mesothelioma patients with occupational history). Asbestos pipe lagging and boiler insulation — primarily amphibole forms — were standard in all ship construction and repair work. [American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine — Istanbul study](https://www.atsjournals.org/doi/full/10.1164/rccm.201810-1922LE)

- **Urban building stock — Istanbul, İzmir, Ankara:** Studies conducted during demolition programs documented that 22–24% of buildings assessed contained asbestos-containing materials. In Istanbul's 2017 demolition survey, 385 of 1,594 demolished buildings (24.2%) tested positive. An Izmir residential building survey (505 samples, 50 buildings) found 22% of buildings positive; chrysotile accounted for 60% of identified fibers. This building stock — primarily constructed between the 1950s and 1990s — represents the ongoing legacy exposure pathway in Turkish cities. [Hürriyet Daily News 2017](https://www.hurriyetdailynews.com/asbestos-in-istanbul-buildings-puts-lives-at-risk-129202); [Springer 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11356-023-31819-4)

## 5. Corporate Responsibility

### Industrial Sector Overview

Unlike Portugal (where three named companies — Lusalite, Cimianto, Novinco — dominated the asbestos-cement industry), Turkey's asbestos industry was more diffuse and no single dominant manufacturer has been prominently named in public-domain English or Turkish-language sources reviewed for this profile. The Turkish asbestos-cement industry served a large domestic construction market across multiple manufacturers. Post-ban, **Atermit Turkey** is documented as a manufacturer that transitioned to non-asbestos fiber-cement products — confirming that the industrial infrastructure previously producing asbestos-cement products was substantial enough to require documented substitution.

- **Atermit Turkey:** Post-2010 fiber-cement sheet manufacturer; documented as a supplier of non-asbestos alternatives to the construction sector following the ban. [IBAS — Substitutes for asbestos-cement construction products](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/bc_subst_asb_cem_constr_prods.php)

### MTA — General Directorate of Mineral Research and Exploration (Maden Tetkik ve Arama)

- **Role:** State geological survey agency established 1935. MTA conducted geological surveys of Turkey's asbestos deposits and published foundational documentation including a 1965 study: *"Asbestos, Magnesite, Sepiolite Deposits of Turkey."* MTA's surveys defined the extent and location of Turkey's chrysotile and tremolite resources.
- **Significance:** By mapping and documenting asbestos deposits, MTA's work facilitated both industrial exploitation and, eventually, environmental health risk assessment. The geological data produced by MTA provided the foundation for identifying high-risk rural areas in the TUNMES-EAECP program.
- **Sources:** [Wikipedia — MTA Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Directorate_of_Mineral_Research_and_Exploration_(Turkey)); [MTA](https://www.mtaic.gov.tr/)

### Occupational Liability Context

No court judgments, compensation settlements, or recognized asbestos-related occupational disease claims have been identified in Turkish public sources for this profile. A 2018 study of Istanbul mesothelioma patients documented that "no recognized compensation claims with mesothelioma" had been identified in the Turkish healthcare system — attributed primarily to low physician awareness of occupational asbestos history-taking and the institutional absence of an occupational disease compensation pathway for asbestos-related cancers. [American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine — Istanbul study (2018)](https://www.atsjournals.org/doi/full/10.1164/rccm.201810-1922LE)

### Context

The near-equal male:female ratio in Turkish mesothelioma (1.36:1 compared to ratios of 6:1 or higher in heavily industrialized countries like the UK or Australia) is the single most revealing data point about Turkey's unique asbestos crisis. In countries where mesothelioma is primarily occupational, men vastly outnumber women because heavy industry was historically male-dominated. Turkey's close-to-equal ratio reflects the dominant role of **environmental and geological exposure** — exposure that affected entire families and village communities regardless of gender, through housing construction, whitewashing, plastering, and daily domestic use of asbestos-bearing local materials.

The mean age at diagnosis (61.7 years) is approximately 10 years younger than comparable European figures (Portugal: 72.1 years), again consistent with earlier and more intense environmental exposure beginning in early childhood in exposed villages, compared to occupational exposure beginning in adulthood in industrialized settings.

The median survival of 8 months and median years of life lost (16.58 for males, 19.83 for females) underscore the devastating prognosis of mesothelioma and the human cost concentrated in communities that had no knowledge that their local soil and building materials were carcinogenic.

## 7. Current Status

- **Environmental exposure continuing:** As of the 2013 TUNMES-EAECP survey, 379 villages were identified with continuing environmental asbestos exposure, affecting approximately 158,068 people. In several villages, the practice of using asbestos-bearing "white soil" as whitewash, insulation, and domestic coating material continued after the 2010 ban, as the ban addresses industrial production and use, not the use of geological materials by villagers. [MDPI — IJERPH (2017)](https://www.mdpi.com/1660-4601/14/11/1293)
- **Village relocations:** Tuzkoy village relocation was completed by 2009. Karain relocation was underway with 141+ new houses built. Sarihidir relocation was proposed or underway as of 2010. The new settlements are built away from erionite-bearing geological formations. Long-term follow-up of relocated populations is ongoing. [Eurasianet](https://eurasianet.org/turkey-end-in-sight-for-cancer-village/); [Washington Post 2010](https://www.washingtonpost.com/wp-dyn/content/article/2010/11/04/AR2010110401287.html)
- **Urban building stock:** Istanbul and İzmir studies (2015–2023) document that approximately 22–24% of pre-ban buildings assessed contain asbestos-containing materials. Demolition without prior asbestos survey remains common in most districts; only a handful of Istanbul districts (Beşiktaş, Şişli, Maltepe, Tuzla, Bağcılar, Kadıköy, Ataşehir) mandate asbestos inventory reports before demolition permits. [Hürriyet Daily News 2017](https://www.hurriyetdailynews.com/asbestos-in-istanbul-buildings-puts-lives-at-risk-129202); [Springer 2023](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11356-023-31819-4)
- **2023 earthquake legacy:** The February 6, 2023 earthquakes generated an estimated 116–200 million tonnes of rubble from pre-ban buildings across ten provinces. Asbestos fibers were released during building collapse and debris removal operations conducted with inadequate protective measures. More than a year post-earthquake, demolition sites near residential areas in Hatay province remained hazardous. International and Turkish public health organizations issued formal risk assessments. [Relief Web 2023](https://reliefweb.int/report/turkiye/secondary-data-review-2023-turkiye-earthquake-asbestos-presence-earthquake-affected-southeast-turkiye-threats-and-suggested-solutions-march-2023); [PMC — Post-earthquake asbestos 2024](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11181204/)
- **No national mesothelioma registry:** Turkey lacks a formal national mesothelioma registry. The TUNMES surveillance data represents a research initiative, not a mandatory reporting system. This gap impedes precise tracking of disease incidence trends. [PubMed — Turkish national surveillance (2017)](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/29068368/)
- **Occupational compensation:** No recognized asbestos-related mesothelioma compensation claims have been documented in Turkish public sources. The occupational disease recognition pathway for asbestos-related cancers is poorly established. [American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine 2018](https://www.atsjournals.org/doi/full/10.1164/rccm.201810-1922LE)
- **Ship recycling:** Workers in Turkish ship dismantling and recycling yards continue to face asbestos exposure risks from vessels built before the global phase-out. Civil society organizations have raised concerns about inadequate protective measures in this sector. [Duvar English 2019](https://www.duvarenglish.com/health-2/2019/12/30/asbestos-continues-to-threaten-public-health-in-turkey)

## Verification Notes

- **Erionite vs. asbestos distinction:** The Cappadocia epidemic is caused primarily by erionite, a fibrous zeolite mineral — this is scientifically established by multiple peer-reviewed sources and IARC classification. The existing countries.json entry for Turkey references "erionite and asbestos" in the 1975 event description; this is acceptable phrasing since both minerals co-occur in Anatolian geology, but the primary carcinogen in the villages is erionite. The distinction matters for scientific accuracy but does not change the broader narrative of Turkey's extraordinary geological carcinogen exposure crisis.
- **"Amiantos SA" / "Eternit Portugal" equivalent in Turkey:** No Turkish equivalent of named dominant manufacturers (analogous to Lusalite or Eternit) was found in reviewed public sources. Turkey's industrial asbestos sector appears to have been more fragmented.
- **Compensation claims:** Absence of documented compensation cases should not be interpreted as absence of harm, but as an institutional and awareness gap documented explicitly in the 2018 AJRCCM study.
- **Source quality:** Core epidemiological data (sections 1, 6) is drawn from peer-reviewed PMC/PubMed sources and the Library of Congress legal monitor. IBAS is used for regulatory chronology cross-reference. News sources (Washington Post, Hürriyet Daily News, Eurasianet) are used only for factual information (village relocations, dates, building surveys) independently corroborated where possible. No data has been fabricated.
