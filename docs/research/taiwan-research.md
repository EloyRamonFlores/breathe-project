### Context

Taiwan's path to a total asbestos ban spanned 37 years — from the first permissible exposure limit in 1981 to the comprehensive prohibition in 2018. The journey was shaped by three critical forces: academic research establishing asbestos-disease correlations, international influences (particularly Japan's 2005 "Kubota Shock" and EU directives), and Taiwan's own industrial transition away from heavy manufacturing toward high-tech.

The 1997 ban on crocidolite and amosite was largely symbolic — since ~95% of consumed asbestos was chrysotile, consumption barely changed after the ban. The real turning point was the 2012 phased schedule, which gave industry specific deadlines. The EPA further accelerated the final brake pad ban by six months, bringing the total ban to January 1, 2018 instead of July.

Despite the comprehensive use ban, Taiwan did not prohibit imports of asbestos-containing products until May 2023 — a five-year gap that required separate regulatory action. The 2025 Typhoon Danas disaster exposed the scale of legacy asbestos still present in aging buildings across central and southern Taiwan, prompting a NT$1.487 billion emergency cleanup budget.

## 2. Historias de Resistencia (Activism Stories)

### Individual Stories

#### Taiwan Occupational Safety and Health Link (TOSHLINK)
- **Who:** NGO focused on occupational health advocacy, led by director Cheng Ya-wen (鄭雅文), professor of Health Policy at National Taiwan University (NTU). PhD in Epidemiology from Harvard School of Public Health (1998).
- **The catalyst:** Growing evidence of asbestos-related disease in Taiwanese workers, combined with the government's failure to act despite established scientific evidence. Bureau of Labor Insurance recognized fewer than 5 asbestos disease cases yearly despite documented exposure of 160,000+ workers.
- **What they did:** Organized campaign workshops (notably December 2015); partnered with international scholars and transnational advocacy networks to build pressure; documented the gap between recognized occupational disease cases and actual prevalence; called for an immediate asbestos ban, medical database for high-risk workers, and re-examination of compensation systems.
- **Impact:** Contributed to the political momentum that led to the 2018 total ban. Highlighted the "invisibility" of asbestos-related diseases in Taiwan's workers' compensation system.
- **Verified quote:** Cheng Ya-wen stated that asbestos "is the primary cause of occupational cancer" and that the situation "is ongoing and on the rise, but the government has not taken proper measures regarding workers' asbestos exposure and disease management." — Source: [Taipei Times, Dec 22, 2015](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2015/12/22/2003635393)
- **Sources:** [Taipei Times](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2015/12/22/2003635393), [PMC5664741](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5664741/), [ResearchGate — Cheng Ya-wen](https://www.researchgate.net/profile/Yawen-Cheng-4)

#### Son Yu-liam (孫友聯) — Taiwan Labor Front
- **Who:** Secretary-General of the Taiwan Labor Front (台灣勞工陣線), a prominent labor rights NGO.
- **The catalyst:** Awareness that asbestos was banned in 50+ nations while Taiwan continued to import over 2,000 tonnes in three years.
- **What they did:** Co-organized the December 2015 press conference and campaign workshop alongside TOSHLINK. Publicly called for an immediate total ban. Drew attention to the international precedent — framing Taiwan as lagging behind global standards.
- **Impact:** Helped build cross-sector coalition between labor groups, public health scholars, and environmental organizations that pressured the EPA to maintain the 2018 ban deadline.
- **Verified quote:** "Asbestos is banned in more than 50 nations, but the material is still legal in Taiwan." — Source: [Taipei Times, Dec 22, 2015](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2015/12/22/2003635393)
- **Sources:** [Taipei Times](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/taiwan/archives/2015/12/22/2003635393)

#### Prof. Jung-Der Wang (王榮德) — Occupational Health Researcher
- **Who:** Professor, Departments of Internal Medicine and Occupational and Environmental Medicine, National Cheng Kung University Hospital, Tainan. Leading researcher on asbestos-related disease in Taiwan.
- **The catalyst:** Decades of research documenting the health impact of asbestos exposure on Taiwanese workers — particularly the 29-year retrospective cohort study of 160,640+ asbestos factory workers.
- **What they did:** Led multiple landmark epidemiological studies: identified clustering of mesothelioma in asbestos cement, thermal insulation, and shipbuilding industries; predicted 659 new mesothelioma cases in the next 30 years; documented gastric cancer risk from asbestos exposure. Co-organized the 2015 campaign workshop with TOSHLINK.
- **Impact:** Provided the scientific evidence base that made the ban politically defensible. Studies demonstrated 5.9-fold to 258-fold increased mesothelioma risk in specific industries.

## 4. Exposure Sources ("Where Was It?")

### Common asbestos-containing materials in Taiwan
Taiwan consumed a total of 668,000 metric tons of asbestos between 1939 and 2015. The material was used extensively in construction, industrial applications, and consumer products.

### Key locations of exposure

- **Mines:** Asbestos deposits discovered on Taiwan's east coast in 1917. Large-scale mining during Japanese colonial era (1937–1944) produced 105–820 metric tons/year. Small-scale domestic mining continued post-WWII but was dwarfed by imports.
  - Source: [PMC5664741](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5664741/), [PubMed 21195893](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21195893/)

- **Factories:** 389 asbestos-related factories identified employing 183,560 workers. Key industries:
  - **Asbestos cement:** Factories in Nantou (central) — extremely high mesothelioma risk (eRR up to 257.9)
  - **Thermal insulation:** eRR of 87.5
  - **Shipbuilding:** 4 factories in Kaohsiung (southern harbor), 1 in Keelung (northern harbor) — 11 of 18 MPM cases came from shipbuilding, with 9 from a single northern facility
  - **Friction materials:** brake linings, clutch plates
  - **Hwa Shin Asbestos Industrial Co., Ltd.:** operated in Kaohsiung
  - Source: [PMC6303649](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6303649/), [PMC4531167](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4531167/)

- **Shipbreaking yards:** Kaohsiung was the world's largest shipbreaking center during 1977–1988, handling approximately 65% of all global obsolete vessels. 4,427 documented shipbreaking workers (1985 Kaohsiung Shipbreaking Workers Union). Workers salvaged war-damaged vessels from WWII — 178 sunken vessels in Kaohsiung harbor, 100+ in Keelung harbor.
  - Source: [PLOS ONE](https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0133128)

- **Products:** Asbestos-cement roofing sheets, flat sheets, roof tiles, brake linings, friction materials, sealants, thermal insulation, pipe lagging, gaskets, textiles. Consumption peaked in 1983 with a secondary peak in 1986 (post-Typhoon Wayne reconstruction).
  - Source: [PMC5664741](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5664741/)

- **Buildings:** The 1960s and 1970s were Taiwan's foundational building boom — four- and five-story apartment buildings (公寓) became the standard urban housing type. Asbestos-cement sheets were widely used in roofing, cladding, and partitions. These buildings remain standing across Taiwan's cities, with no nationwide inventory or labeling system for residual asbestos.
  - Source: [Taipei Times, Mar 28, 2024](https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/feat/archives/2024/03/28/2003815572), [PMC5664741](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5664741/)

### Context

Taiwan faces a predicted mesothelioma epidemic. With 668,000 tonnes of asbestos consumed and a median latency of 33.2 years, peak incidence was predicted for 2012–2020 (males) and 2016–2020 (females). The predicted 659 new cases over 2017–2046 is likely conservative, as the study noted data limitations in earlier decades.

A critical issue is the extreme underrecognition of occupational asbestos disease. The Bureau of Labor Insurance recognized fewer than 5 cases per year despite 160,000+ documented workers in asbestos factories. This gap — documented by Cheng & Huang (2022) in their study "Explaining the Invisibility of Asbestos-Related Diseases in the Taiwan Workers' Compensation System" — reflects systematic barriers to occupational disease recognition.

Before ICD-10 adoption in 2008, Taiwan had no specific mesothelioma code, forcing deaths to be classified under 14 different ICD-9 codes. This coding challenge contributed to underreporting of mesothelioma mortality.

## 6. Current Status (What's Happening NOW?)

- **Is the ban being enforced?** YES. Customs border inspections for asbestos-containing imports reinforced since June 2023. Inter-departmental management review held November 2024. No permits for asbestos manufacturing issued since January 1, 2018.
  - Source: [CHA.gov.tw](https://www.cha.gov.tw/cp-193-6596-ca1fe-2.html), [Focus Taiwan](https://focustaiwan.tw/business/202210040030)

- **Ongoing removal programs?** YES. Government subsidizes asbestos roof removal at NT$45,000/ton. Program expanded in June 2024 to include commercial and shuttered industrial buildings (previously only residential/non-commercial). After Typhoon Danas (2025), Executive Yuan allocated NT$1.487 billion (special budget) for emergency asbestos waste removal — 27,978 metric tons cleared by January 12, 2026.
  - Source: [CHA.gov.tw](https://www.cha.gov.tw/cp-193-7031-7feae-2.html)

- **Pending legislation?** No major new legislation pending. The regulatory framework (TCSCA total ban + import ban) is comprehensive. Focus has shifted to enforcement and legacy asbestos management.

- **Active lawsuits?** No major ongoing asbestos-related lawsuits identified in public sources. The lack of litigation reflects the systemic underrecognition of occupational asbestos disease, not an absence of victims.

- **Recent news:** The 2025 Typhoon Danas disaster scattered aging asbestos roofing debris across central and southern Taiwan, creating a post-disaster environmental crisis. Ministry of Environment deployed aerial imagery, remote sensing (with National Cheng Kung University), and a citizen reporting mobile app (developed by Goodideas-Studio) to locate and prioritize asbestos waste removal.
  - Source: [CHA.gov.tw](https://www.cha.gov.tw/cp-193-7031-7feae-2.html)

## Verification Notes

- **Ban year discrepancy:** The current countries.json lists ban_year as 2019. Research confirms the total ban took effect January 1, 2018 (IBAS, PMC5664741, ChemLinked). The "February 1, 2019" date may refer to a specific import restriction, but the substantive ban date is 2018. **Correcting to 2018.**
- **Mesothelioma rate per 100,000:** No clean age-standardized rate per 100,000 was found for Taiwan in recent sources. The 907 cumulative cases over 35 years in a population of ~23 million suggests a low rate, consistent with Taiwan being described as having "much lower than expected" DALYs for its sociodemographic index. This may reflect underdiagnosis/undercoding rather than truly low incidence.
- **Corporate names:** The epidemiological studies anonymize factory identities. Only Hwa Shin Asbestos Industrial Co., Ltd. was identified by name. Human follow-up could identify more companies through Bureau of Labor Insurance records.
- **Japanese colonial mining data:** Mining output figures (105–820 tonnes/year, 1937–1944) come from the PMC article; original Japanese colonial records should be verified.
- **Compensation system:** The extreme gap between documented exposure (160,000+ workers) and recognized disease (<5 cases/year) is one of the starkest findings. This needs human follow-up to determine if reforms have occurred since the 2022 Cheng & Huang publication.
