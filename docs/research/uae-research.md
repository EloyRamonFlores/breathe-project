**Researcher:** Claude (ToxinFree)
**Date:** 2026-04-07
**Status:** Draft — Pending human review
**Confidence:** MEDIUM — The UAE's regulatory framework is documented in official federal and emirate-level instruments, and construction sector exposure data is well-documented. Key gaps: no national mesothelioma registry; no public-domain data on individual occupational compensation cases; IBAS does not list the UAE as a full-ban country due to ongoing asbestos-cement pipe production. The most important nuance in this profile is the **partial ban**: Federal Cabinet Resolution 39/2006 banned asbestos panels (boards) but explicitly left asbestos-cement pipes unregulated — and those pipes were actively manufactured and widely used in infrastructure for years after 2006.

## 1. Regulatory Timeline

The UAE's asbestos regulatory history reflects a construction economy that embraced the material from the 1960s through the 1990s during its oil-boom urbanization, and then moved toward partial restriction — but not a coherent total ban — in the mid-2000s. The critical gap that defined the UAE's asbestos story is structural: the 2006 Federal Cabinet Resolution banned the import and production of asbestos *panels and boards* but left asbestos-cement *pipes* — used throughout water distribution and sewerage infrastructure — entirely unregulated.

### Context

The UAE's relationship with asbestos mirrors the broader pattern of rapid Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) development: the material was embraced without restriction during the construction boom of the 1960s–1990s, then partially restricted once international awareness grew — but the restriction was incomplete and imperfectly enforced. The 2006 Federal Cabinet Resolution addressed the most visible asbestos product (flat and corrugated asbestos-cement boards used for roofing and cladding) while leaving asbestos-cement pipes — used in water distribution and sewerage — entirely untouched. This gap allowed asbestos-cement pipe manufacturing to continue legally in the UAE well into the 2010s.

The workforce most exposed was never the UAE's relatively small national population. The UAE construction sector employed over 500,000 workers at the peak of its boom, the overwhelming majority of whom were migrant workers from South Asia. These workers built the towers, villas, and infrastructure of modern Dubai, Abu Dhabi, and Sharjah while handling asbestos-containing materials without adequate protection and in conditions documented by Human Rights Watch as exploitative and unregulated. No compensation framework for asbestos-related disease has been identified in UAE public sources for these workers.

Not a single country in the Middle East has ratified ILO Convention No. 162 (Asbestos Convention, 1986), which establishes the minimum international standard for worker protection from asbestos exposure. The UAE is among those non-ratifiers.

## 4. Exposure Sources ("Where Was It?")

### Common Asbestos-Containing Materials in UAE

The UAE construction sector used the full range of asbestos-containing materials standard to the global construction industry of the 1960s–1990s. Given the Gulf climate's requirements for roofing, insulation, and water distribution, certain product categories were dominant.

- **Asbestos-cement corrugated roofing sheets:** The most ubiquitous product in low-rise residential construction. Used in villas, workers' camps, agricultural buildings, car park shade structures, and light industrial facilities throughout the UAE. Prevalent from the 1960s through the early 2000s.
- **Asbestos-cement flat sheets (cladding and partitions):** Interior and exterior cladding, soffit boards, and partition walls in commercial and residential buildings from the 1960s–1990s.
- **Asbestos-cement water pipes:** The most significant unresolved legacy exposure pathway. Used in municipal water distribution and sewerage networks throughout UAE cities. The 2006 federal ban did not address these pipes; asbestos-cement pipe factories continued operating in the UAE after 2006, manufacturing pipes still used in public infrastructure.
- **Pipe lagging and boiler insulation:** Thermal insulation on hot water systems, steam pipes, and HVAC equipment in industrial facilities and large buildings. Typically amphibole-based insulation was imported for higher-temperature applications.
- **Gaskets and seals:** Asbestos gaskets in industrial machinery, vehicle engines, and plumbing connections. These continued to be imported after the 2006 panel ban, as the ban's scope did not clearly cover gasket-form asbestos products.
- **Asbestos textiles (gloves, blankets):** Used in industrial and construction applications for fire protection.
- **Ceiling tiles and acoustic panels:** Asbestos-containing ceiling tiles installed in older commercial and institutional buildings.
- **Asbestos cement pipe products:** The most egregious legacy item — still being actively manufactured in UAE factories after 2006, with 17,000+ tonnes of chrysotile imported in 2007 alone for this purpose.

### Key Locations of Exposure

- **Dubai — construction sites (1970s–2000s):** Dubai's rapid skyline development — World Trade Centre (1979), then the Burj Al Arab (1999), the Palm Jumeirah, Downtown Dubai — spanned four decades during which migrant workers handled asbestos without adequate protection. The 500,000+ construction workers employed at peak construction were overwhelmingly from India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh. Workers' camps attached to construction sites housed workers in conditions documented by HRW as dangerous and exploitative, with no occupational health monitoring.
- **Abu Dhabi — infrastructure and residential construction:** Capital city development and residential villa construction. Abu Dhabi's pattern mirrors Dubai's: large-scale asbestos-cement use in villas and infrastructure, now creating a legacy building stock challenge.
- **Sharjah, Ajman, Ras Al Khaimah — lower-income residential areas:** The northern emirates, with lower construction standards and older building stock, contain a higher proportion of asbestos-cement roofing. Rental villas in these emirates — housing lower-income migrant workers and laborers — frequently have asbestos-cement roofing.
- **Industrial zones:** JAFZA (Jebel Ali Free Zone), industrial zones in Abu Dhabi, and manufacturing facilities across the UAE housed industries where asbestos insulation and gaskets were standard.
- **Water distribution infrastructure (nationwide):** Asbestos-cement water pipes are documented in the public water supply and sewerage networks of multiple UAE cities. These pipes, installed over decades and still in place, represent an ongoing exposure pathway during maintenance, repair, and eventual replacement.

## 5. Corporate Responsibility

### Asbestos-Cement Pipe Manufacturers

The UAE's most significant corporate accountability issue is the continued operation of asbestos-cement pipe manufacturing facilities *within the UAE* after the 2006 partial ban. Specific company names for UAE-based asbestos-cement pipe manufacturers have not been identified in public-domain English-language sources reviewed for this profile. However, the existence of such manufacturers is positively documented: "More than 17,000 tonnes of asbestos were imported into the country for the manufacture of asbestos cement pipes, which are used to transport tap water and sewerage" — confirming active manufacturing presence. [ME Construction News](https://meconstructionnews.com/31344/asbestos-fighting-a-silent-killer-in-the-uae)

### Import and Distribution Networks

The IBAS "Preventing Asbestos Imports" analysis documents that, despite the 2006 ban on panels, "some suppliers in the Middle East are still selling building materials that contain asbestos" — imported through channels that bypass UAE customs inspection. Chrysotile asbestos in gasket and insulation form has been documented entering the UAE market after 2006.

### No Documented Litigation

No court judgments, compensation settlements, or occupational disease claims related to asbestos in UAE have been identified in public-domain sources. The absence of litigation reflects the UAE's limited public litigation culture for occupational disease, particularly by migrant workers who lack the legal standing, resources, and residency stability to pursue such claims.

### Context

The UAE's mesothelioma burden is under-documented for structural reasons. The population most exposed — migrant construction workers — is not captured in UAE health statistics after they return to their countries of origin (India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka). A UAE worker exposed to asbestos in the 1980s–2000s who develops mesothelioma after the typical 20–50 year latency period would likely be diagnosed and die in their home country, not in the UAE. This means UAE national health statistics systematically fail to capture the true burden of the country's asbestos legacy. The construction boom that built modern Dubai and Abu Dhabi exported its asbestos mortality to South Asia.

The declining mesothelioma trend within UAE itself (per GBD 2021) likely reflects both the partial ban and, importantly, the fact that the affected population (migrant workers) is no longer in the country to be counted. It does not indicate the legacy has been resolved — it indicates it has been exported.

## 7. Current Status

- **Legal gap on pipes:** As of the most recent verified information, no federal regulation bans asbestos-cement water pipes in the UAE. The manufacture and use of these pipes remains in a legal grey zone. Abu Dhabi's OSHAD-SF CoP 1.10 (updated 2026) governs *management of* ACMs but does not constitute a ban on new asbestos-cement pipe installation.
- **Enforcement gaps on panels:** Despite the 2006 ban on asbestos panels, enforcement has been consistently documented as inadequate. Asbestos-containing materials have been found in new buildings (offices, schools, power stations) years after the ban. Black-market recycling of asbestos materials has been documented.
- **Dubai Municipality guidelines (2022):** Technical Guidelines GU50-SHA2 Version 3.0 (February 2022) govern safe handling, demolition, transport, and disposal of asbestos in Dubai. These represent an active management framework but do not address the ban gaps.
- **Abu Dhabi OSHAD CoP 1.10 (updated 2026):** The most current version of Abu Dhabi's ACM management code. Requires asbestos surveys and licensed removal before demolition.
- **No ILO Convention 162 ratification:** The UAE — like all Middle East countries — has not ratified ILO Convention No. 162 (Asbestos Convention, 1986), establishing minimum international standards for worker protection.
- **Legacy building stock:** The UAE's building stock contains extensive asbestos-containing materials in pre-2006 buildings, with ongoing exposure risks during demolition, renovation, and maintenance. Rapid urban transformation (demolition and replacement of older buildings) continues to create exposure events.
- **No compensation framework:** No mesothelioma or asbestosis compensation fund or recognized occupational disease pathway exists for current or former construction workers affected by asbestos exposure in the UAE.

1. **Pipe manufacturing companies:** The specific names of UAE-based asbestos-cement pipe manufacturers are not available in public-domain English-language sources. This is the most critical research gap in this profile. Human follow-up via FOI or direct industry inquiry is needed.
2. **Ban year ambiguity:** The 2006 Federal Cabinet Resolution No. 39 is consistently cited but the original Arabic-language text was not retrieved in this research. The characterization of its exact scope (panels only vs. broader) is based on secondary English-language sources. A human researcher should retrieve and verify the original Arabic text.
3. **2007 import data (17,000 tonnes):** Cited via Anthesis Middle East blog referencing USGS data. The USGS Minerals Yearbook 2007 should be verified directly for this figure.
4. **"344 times higher" mortality statistic:** This statistic appears in the Anthesis Middle East blog without a specific citation to its original source. It should be traced to its primary source (likely a WHO or peer-reviewed occupational health study).
5. **Migrant worker health outcomes:** The structural opacity of migrant worker health outcomes (exposure in UAE, disease and death in country of origin) means the full health burden of UAE asbestos exposure is genuinely unquantifiable from UAE-based sources. This is not a research gap that can be filled with more UAE-source searching — it requires cross-referencing with occupational health data from India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh.
6. **ILO Convention 162 non-ratification:** Confirmed via ILO sources that no Middle East country has ratified Convention 162.

## Suggested Improvements

- Retrieve and verify the Arabic text of Federal Cabinet Resolution No. 39/2006 to confirm exact scope.
- Identify specific asbestos-cement pipe manufacturers operating in UAE post-2006 via UAE Ministry of Industry trade registrations or FOI.
- Cross-reference mesothelioma registries in India, Pakistan, and Bangladesh for workers who returned after UAE construction employment — this is where UAE's asbestos mortality is likely documented.
- Contact ADPHC or UAE Ministry of Health for mortality data on asbestos-related diseases in UAE nationals (as distinct from migrant workers).
- Investigate whether the 2006 ban has been formally expanded to cover pipes in any subsequent regulatory update not captured in English-language sources.
