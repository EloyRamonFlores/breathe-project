## 1. Regulatory Timeline

France was a major asbestos producer, importer, and consumer throughout the 20th century. Despite documented evidence of asbestos hazards dating to the early 1900s, meaningful regulation was not introduced until 1977 — 46 years after the UK and 31 years after the US. The 1997 complete ban came after a damaging 1996 public health report and two decades of determined organizing by workers, academics, and victims.

### Context

France's regulatory timeline reveals a pattern of industry control over scientific information and government policy. Saint-Gobain and Eternit, the two dominant French asbestos manufacturers, jointly created the Comité Permanent Amiante (Permanent Asbestos Committee) — a public relations body that systematically suppressed adverse scientific findings and spread industry-funded propaganda from the 1980s onward. This committee worked to prevent meaningful regulation for nearly 15 years after the risks were widely known within the industry.

The 1997 ban was a turning point not just for France but for the global ban movement. France was the first major industrial nation to unilaterally prohibit chrysotile. Canada challenged the ban at the WTO; France won in 2000. The ruling provided a crucial legal precedent that other nations could ban asbestos without violating free trade obligations.

## 2. Historias de Resistencia (Activism Stories)

### Henri Pézerat (1928–2009) — Scientist, Whistleblower, and Founder of the French Anti-Asbestos Movement

- **Who:** Research director and toxicologist at the CNRS (Centre National de la Recherche Scientifique), based at the Jussieu campus in Paris. Specialized in fiber toxicity and carcinogenesis.
- **The catalyst:** In 1973, while working in his Jussieu chemistry laboratory, Pézerat identified asbestos in the white dust falling from the ceiling. After examining the scientific literature on chrysotile's carcinogenic properties, he reported this to an inter-union safety collective — launching what would become a 35-year campaign.
- **What he did:**
  - Coordinated the first anti-asbestos actions at Jussieu University in 1975, including strikes, assemblies, and negotiations.
  - Built coalitions between student protesters, trade unions, and asbestos factory workers (Ferodo, Amisol).
  - Co-founded ANDEVA (Association Nationale de Défense des Victimes de l'Amiante) in 1995.
  - Founded Ban Asbestos France in 1995, which joined with ANDEVA to create the political pressure that produced the 1997 ban.
  - Published scientific papers disproving the industry claim that chrysotile was safe, including a posthumous paper *Chrysotile Biopersistance — The Misuse of Biased Studies* demolishing a key industry argument.
  - Received the June Hancock Award in 2000 for his international contribution to the global ban campaign.
- **Impact:** The Jussieu campaign he started in 1975 is now credited as the founding moment of the French anti-asbestos movement. France's 1997 ban is directly traceable to the pressure his networks generated. His Ban Asbestos France work contributed to the EU-wide chrysotile ban in 2005. The Association Henri Pézerat continues his work. He died February 16, 2009.

### ANDEVA (Association Nationale de Défense des Victimes de l'Amiante) — France's National Asbestos Victims Association

- **Who:** National advocacy organization founded in 1995 (some sources say 1996), bringing together three distinct constituencies: textile workers from the Amisol asbestos factory (Clermont-Ferrand), teachers and students from Jussieu University, and trade union representatives.
- **The catalyst:** The convergence of the Jussieu campus contamination crisis, the Amisol workers' exposure campaign, and the 1995–1996 momentum toward a national ban created the conditions for a unified national organization.
- **What they did:**
  - Created the political and media pressure that, combined with the 1996 INSERM report, produced the government's decision to ban asbestos by January 1, 1997.
  - Led the campaign for FIVA's creation — ensuring that the compensation fund covered environmental and domestic exposure, not just occupational cases.
  - Built from the original founding members to 32 local associations, 14,600 members and donors, and 96 committed volunteers.
  - Documented 21,350 cases of employer gross negligence (faute inexcusable).
  - In 2023, demanded emergency protective measures after an investigative documentary revealed over 700,000 students in contaminated schools.
- **Impact:** ANDEVA is the primary force behind France's asbestos victim support system. The FIVA fund, which has paid over €6.7 billion in cumulative compensation since 2001, exists largely because of ANDEVA's campaign. Their model of combining victims' mutual aid with legal advocacy has been replicated globally.

## 3. Exposure Sources ("Where Was It?")

### Common Asbestos-Containing Materials in France

France operated two domestic asbestos mines (closed in 1962), then imported primarily Canadian chrysotile for the rest of the century. France was Europe's leading consumer of chrysotile — in 1996, the year before the ban, France imported 29,978 metric tons from Canada alone. Asbestos-cement products dominated consumption throughout the 20th century.

**Key product types specific to France:**
- **Eternit-brand asbestos-cement products:** Roofing sheets, flat sheets, pipes, guttering, and facade panels. Eternit established French production from 1903.
- **Sprayed asbestos insulation ("flocage"):** Applied to metal structural elements in public buildings, schools, hospitals, and universities for fire protection. This was the source of the Jussieu University contamination — the process created friable asbestos that degraded and released fibers into the air.
- **Flooring:** Vinyl-asbestos tiles, widely used in commercial and institutional buildings.
- **Friction materials:** Brake linings and clutch pads manufactured by Ferodo and Valeo in Condé-sur-Noireau (Normandy).
- **Pipe insulation and lagging:** Used in heating systems throughout commercial and residential buildings.

### Key Locations of Exposure

**Factories:**
- **Amisol (Clermont-Ferrand):** Asbestos spinning and textile factory. Workers at Amisol joined with Jussieu University activists in 1975 — their coalition was a foundational moment for the French movement. The factory closed in 1974. The Paris Court of Appeal dismissed the investigation into Amisol contamination. Source: [IBAS — France Profile](https://ibasecretariat.org/prof_france.php), [Batinfo — Asbestos France procedures](https://batinfo.com/en/actuality/asbestos-in-france-many-procedures-and-non-places_23471)
- **Ferodo-Valéo (Condé-sur-Noireau, Calvados, Normandy):** Automotive friction material factory. More than 300 workers suffered from asbestos-related occupational diseases. Former French Labour Minister Martine Aubry was indicted in 2012 for her role (while Director of Labour Relations at the Ministry, 1984–1987) in failing to prevent exposures. Investigating judges dismissed the case. Source: [WSWS — Ferodo-Valeo](https://www.wsws.org/en/articles/2012/11/aubr-n17.html), [Tellerreport — Dismissal](https://www.tellerreport.com/news/2019-07-18---asbestos-scandal--dismissal-of-the-judges-in-the-case-of-ferodo-valeo-de-cond%C3%A9-sur-noireau-.rJeALVZ0-B.html)
- **Turner & Newall (Normandy):** The British asbestos giant established a friction material factory in Normandy in the 1930s specifically to exploit France's lack of asbestos regulation. Source: [IBAS — France Profile](https://ibasecretariat.org/prof_france.php)
- **Eternit and Saint-Gobain plants:** Multiple manufacturing locations across France. Both companies were indicted for homicide and involuntary injury in separate proceedings. Source: [IBAS — France Profile](https://ibasecretariat.org/prof_france.php)

**Buildings:**
- **Jussieu University campus (Paris, 5th arrondissement):** Described as "Europe's biggest asbestos-contaminated site." Sprayed asbestos insulation was applied to metal structural elements during construction. First detected in 1973 by Henri Pézerat. A 1995 flood in the physics laboratory caused major asbestos dispersion. Decontamination operations extended from the 1990s into the 2010s. Source: [IBAS — Jussieu Judicial Scandal](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-asbestos-contamination-at-jussieu-university-a-judicial-scandal.php)
- **French schools:** As of 2023, more than one-third of French schools contain asbestos. In Paris, nearly 70% of public schools (440 of 644) tested positive for asbestos contamination. In Île-de-France, 90% of tested schools contained asbestos, with 40% exceeding US safety standards. An estimated 60 teachers per year developed mesothelioma between 1998–2017. Source: [IBAS — Asbestos Scandal in France 2023](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-asbestos-scandal-in-france-2023.php)

### Ferodo-Valéo (Condé-sur-Noireau)

- **Role:** Manufacturer of asbestos friction materials (brake linings, clutch pads) in Normandy.

### Context

France's mesothelioma pattern has a distinct gender dimension. Male mesothelioma incidence has been declining slightly since the early 2000s peak, reflecting the 1977 occupational exposure limits and the 1997 ban. Female incidence, however, is increasing — attributed to non-occupational exposures (domestic, environmental, and school/workplace building contamination rather than direct industrial work). The 40% rate of occupational exposure among women with mesothelioma (versus 90%+ for men) suggests a large burden of undocumented environmental exposure.

The FIVA fund represents France's most significant policy achievement: as one of the few countries in the world to provide comprehensive no-fault compensation regardless of whether exposure was occupational or environmental, France has set an international standard. The €6.7 billion in cumulative payments since 2001 reflects the scale of the epidemic.

## 6. Current Status (What's Happening NOW?)

**Is the ban being enforced?**
Yes. The 1997 ban (Decree 96-1133) is fully enforced. Import, manufacture, and use of all asbestos forms including chrysotile are prohibited. Source: [Anses — Asbestos Still Topical](https://www.anses.fr/en/content/asbestos-still-very-topical-problem)

**Schools crisis (2023):**
A June 2023 France 5 documentary investigation found asbestos in more than one-third of French schools — affecting approximately 700,000 students. In Paris, 70% of public schools tested positive; in Île-de-France, 40% exceeded US safety standards. The Ministry of National Education stated it "did not have up-to-date information on asbestos in schools." ANDEVA called for emergency protective measures. MP Louis Boyard requested a parliamentary commission of inquiry. Source: [IBAS — Asbestos Scandal in France 2023](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-asbestos-scandal-in-france-2023.php)

**Active litigation:**
- **Jussieu University case:** July 5, 2023, the Paris Court of Appeal upheld dismissal of criminal charges related to asbestos exposures at Jussieu (1960–1990). Two secondary points were referred back to investigating judges. Victims' groups pledged Supreme Court appeal. Source: [IBAS — Jussieu Judicial Scandal](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-asbestos-contamination-at-jussieu-university-a-judicial-scandal.php)
- **CMMP case (2024):** The Paris Court of Appeal condemned the CMMP company to pay over €13 million to the Municipality of Aulnay-sous-Bois and three associations — a significant 2024 legal victory for asbestos contamination claims. Source: [Association Henri Pézerat — CMMP ruling](https://www.asso-henri-pezerat.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/12/COMMENTAIRE-DECISION-JUSTICE-DOSSIER-CMMP-V2-1.pdf)

**Ongoing removal:**
France has no nationally mandated asbestos removal program with dedicated funding. The "manage in place" approach is standard for undamaged asbestos. Removal is required for damaged materials or during renovation/demolition. The asbestos removal market in France is estimated at several hundred million euros annually and is strictly regulated under the Code de la Santé Publique. Source: [businesscoot — Asbestos Removal Market France 2024](https://www.businesscoot.com/en/study/the-asbestos-removal-market-france)
