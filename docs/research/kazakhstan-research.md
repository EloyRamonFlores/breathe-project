## 1. Ban Status Verification

### Regulatory Status: **no_ban**

Kazakhstan has **no ban on any form of asbestos**. It is the world's second-largest chrysotile asbestos producer and exporter. The country actively opposes international restrictions on chrysotile trade.

### Verification Checklist

1. **Is this country on the IBAS full ban list?**
   - **NO.** Kazakhstan is not on the [IBAS ban list](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/alpha_ban_list.php). It is listed as a major producing country.

2. **What specific asbestos forms are banned?**
   - **None.** No form of asbestos is banned in Kazakhstan. Chrysotile is actively mined and exported. No legislation restricting crocidolite or amosite was found either — Kazakhstan mines only chrysotile, and all forms remain legal.
   - Source: [IBAS — What is going on in Kazakhstan?](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-what-is-going-on-in-kazakhstan.php)

3. **Is chrysotile specifically addressed?**
   - **Yes — it is actively promoted.** Chrysotile is the foundation of Kazakhstan's asbestos industry. The government and industry promote "controlled use" as safe. Kazakhstan co-funds the International Chrysotile Association (ICA) to lobby against international restrictions.
   - Source: [IBAS — The world is now waiting for Kazakhstani chrysotile](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-the-world-is-waiting-for-kazakhstani-chrysotile.php)

4. **What year did each restriction take effect?**
   - **No restrictions have taken effect.** Kazakhstan has workplace exposure limits (Maximum Permissible Concentration of 0.5 mg/m³ for dust containing >20% asbestos; 1 mg/m³ for <20%), but these are regulatory limits on dust concentration, not restrictions on asbestos use, production, or trade. Studies show these limits are routinely exceeded — daily dust concentrations of 6 mg/m³ were measured in 2014 (12x the limit).
   - Source: [PMC — Human Exposure to Asbestos in Central Asian Countries](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11734637/)

5. **Is there evidence of enforcement?**
   - **No enforcement of restrictions because no restrictions exist.** Workplace dust limits are poorly enforced. In October 2024, the Ministry of Ecology issued instructions for inspections of industrial facilities in Zhitikara following public mobilization over cancer incidence, but this was a response to citizen complaints, not proactive enforcement.
   - Source: [IBAS News Archive — Kazakhstan](https://ibasecretariat.org/abs_archive_news.php?sel=c&c_val=Kazakhstan)

6. **Internal consistency check:**
   - `ban_status: "no_ban"` ✓ — matches all evidence: active production, no legislation, government promotion of chrysotile
   - `ban_details` text: consistent with no_ban
   - Timeline events: all are type `"other"` or `"regulation"` (workplace limits) — none are `"ban"` or `"partial_ban"`

### ⚠️ Note on False "2019 Ban" Claim

One secondary source ([asbestosclaims.law](https://www.asbestosclaims.law/asbestos-blog/whats-next-for-asbestos-legislation/)) claims "A law passed in 2019 banned the use and production of these products effective January 2021." **This claim is demonstrably false.** IBAS documents continued production of 248,000–250,000 tonnes in 2021, Q1 2023 production of 66,500 tonnes (21.9% increase year-over-year), and a new asbestos-cement façade panel factory opening in October 2024. Kazakhstan's delegation led the Rotterdam Convention veto against chrysotile listing in June 2022 and May 2023. No credible source (IBAS, USGS, WHO, or Kazakh government) supports this claim.

### Context

Kazakhstan's relationship with asbestos is fundamentally different from most countries in the ToxinFree database. Rather than a story of discovery, harm, and eventual ban, Kazakhstan's narrative is one of active production, export, and defense of the chrysotile industry as an economic cornerstone.

The Zhitikara deposit in the Kostanay region holds an estimated 37 million tonnes of chrysotile reserves — the fourth-largest in the world. Kostanay Minerals JSC, the country's sole producer, has operated continuously since 1965 and produced over 17.5 million tonnes cumulatively. In 2021, annual output reached 248,000–250,000 tonnes, with 95–98% exported to countries in Central and Southeast Asia.

The 2009 Astana Asbestos Resolution — adopted at the first public conference to openly discuss chrysotile health risks in Kazakhstan — represented a rare moment of dissent. But the government and industry response has been to double down: organizing pro-asbestos rallies, funding the International Chrysotile Association, leading Rotterdam Convention vetoes, and even deploying corporate espionage against anti-asbestos activists (see Section 4).

The most significant recent development is the October 2024 mobilization by Zhitikara residents demanding environmental disaster zone recognition due to elevated cancer rates — the first grassroots public health challenge to the asbestos industry from within Kazakhstan.

## 3. Historias de Resistencia (Activism Stories)

### Organizational Advocacy

#### GreenWomen / WECF Coalition (2009–2011)

- **Who:** GreenWomen is a Kazakh environmental NGO. They partnered with WECF (Women in Europe for a Common Future), Eco-Forum, and EcoCenter — a coalition of Kazakh civil society organizations.
- **The catalyst:** The complete absence of public debate about chrysotile health risks in Kazakhstan, despite the country being the world's third-largest producer at the time (~220,000 tonnes/year).
- **What they did:** Organized the First International Asbestos Conference in Astana (April 20–21, 2009) — the first public open debate in Kazakhstan's history on chrysotile health risks. 75 participants from government, industry, and civil society attended. Speakers included Dr. Ivan Ivanov (WHO), European ambassadors, and Dr. Donald Cooper (Rotterdam Convention Executive Secretary). The conference produced the **Astana Asbestos Resolution**, unanimously adopted, calling on the Kazakh government to provide transparency, develop a national asbestos profile, and discuss chrysotile listing in the Rotterdam Convention.
- **Impact:** Broke the silence on asbestos health risks in a producing country. The resolution was the first formal domestic call for chrysotile regulation in Kazakhstan. WECF continued follow-up work through 2011, conducting environmental sampling that confirmed carcinogenic exposure risks in construction materials.

#### Zhitikara Residents (2024–present)

- **Who:** Residents of Zhitikara, the asbestos mining town in Kostanay region (population ~40,000), adjacent to the Kostanay Minerals open-pit mine.
- **The catalyst:** Elevated cancer incidence in the region. Zhitikara has the fourth-highest cancer incidence rate in the Kostanay region, which itself has a lung cancer rate of 32.5 per 100,000 — among the highest in Kazakhstan.
- **What they did:** Mobilized publicly to demand that Zhitikara be recognized as an environmental disaster zone. Their pressure prompted the Ministry of Ecology to issue instructions for inspections of industrial facilities in the area (October 2024).
- **Impact:** First grassroots public health challenge to the asbestos industry from within a mining community in Kazakhstan. The Ministry of Ecology response (inspections) represents a rare official acknowledgment of environmental concerns. Outcome of inspections is pending as of early 2026.

### Note on Limited Activism Data

Kazakhstan's asbestos industry has actively suppressed dissent. In 2016, it was revealed that Kusto Group (majority shareholder of Kostanay Minerals) funded a corporate espionage operation ("Project Spring") through K2 Intelligence to infiltrate the global anti-asbestos movement between 2012–2016 (see Section 4). An operative, Robert Moore, posed as a documentary filmmaker to surveil activists including IBAS coordinator Laurie Kazan-Allen. This climate of surveillance and intimidation helps explain why individual named activists are difficult to identify in Kazakhstan. The 2024 Zhitikara residents' mobilization is significant precisely because it represents a rare act of public resistance from within a mining community.

## 4. Exposure Sources ("Where Was It?")

### Common Asbestos-Containing Materials in Kazakhstan

Kazakhstan both produces and consumes chrysotile asbestos products. An estimated 50% of Kazakhstani homes are roofed with asbestos-containing materials. Domestic consumption is relatively low compared to production — only 7,000–15,000 tonnes/year are consumed domestically (vs. 220,000–260,000 tonnes produced), but the legacy of Soviet-era construction means asbestos is ubiquitous in the built environment.

**Key product types:**
- **Asbestos-cement roofing sheets** — most widespread domestic use; found on residential, industrial, and agricultural buildings across the country
- **Asbestos-cement flat sheets** — used in interior and exterior wall cladding
- **Asbestos-cement water pipes** — used in municipal water infrastructure
- **Pipe insulation** — lagging on heating systems in buildings constructed during the Soviet era
- **Asbestos gaskets** — industrial applications
- **Asbestos-cement façade panels** — new product line launched in October 2024 at a new manufacturing facility

Sources: [IBAS News Archive](https://ibasecretariat.org/abs_archive_news.php?sel=c&c_val=Kazakhstan), [PMC — Human Exposure to Asbestos in Central Asian Countries](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11734637/)

### Key Locations of Exposure

**Mines:**
- **Zhitikara (Zhetikara) open-pit mine** — Kostanay region, northern Kazakhstan. The sole asbestos mine in Kazakhstan and Central Asia. Operated by Kostanay Minerals JSC since 1965. The Zhitikarinskoye deposit holds 37 million tonnes of chrysotile reserves, ranking 4th globally. Open-pit mining method. The quarry is located directly adjacent to the town of Zhitikara (~40,000 residents), creating both occupational and environmental exposure.
- Source: [Nature — Zhitikara deposit](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-024-83239-0), [IBAS](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-what-is-going-on-in-kazakhstan.php)

**Factories:**
- **Kostanay Minerals JSC processing complex** — ore crushing and enrichment facility adjacent to the mine. 215 mining workers and 311 processing workers. Processes raw chrysotile ore into fiber grades for export and domestic use.
- **Asbestos-cement product manufacturers** — approximately 6,000 additional workers employed in downstream asbestos-cement companies across Kazakhstan.
- **New façade panel factory** (opened October 2024) — Kazakhstan's first facility for manufacturing asbestos-cement façade panels, part of government strategy to increase domestic consumption.
- Sources: [PMC — Immune Status of Workers](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9655464/), [IBAS News Archive](https://ibasecretariat.org/abs_archive_news.php?sel=c&c_val=Kazakhstan)

**Environmental exposure:**
- Zhitikara town — the city sits directly adjacent to the open-pit mine. Residents face environmental exposure from airborne dust. An autopsy study of 343 deceased found black dust particles in 44.6% of non-occupationally-exposed Zhitikara residents, compared to 33.3% of Kostanay Minerals workers — suggesting significant environmental contamination.
- **Asbestos waste dumps** — uncontrolled dumping of asbestos-containing waste reported by local NGOs and EU partners (2010 WECF seminar findings). German laboratory analysis confirmed carcinogenic exposure risks.
- Sources: [PMC — Human Exposure](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11734637/), [IBAS News Archive](https://ibasecretariat.org/abs_archive_news.php?sel=c&c_val=Kazakhstan)

**Export routes (exposure extends beyond Kazakhstan):**
- Primary: India ($67M), China ($38.5M), Indonesia ($22M), Sri Lanka ($9.4M), Vietnam ($9.4M), Uzbekistan ($7.8M), Thailand ($7.6M), Bangladesh ($6.5M) — 2020 export values
- Post-2022: New routes through Georgian ports (Poti, Batumi) and China-Laos-Thailand rail link after Russian ports became unavailable due to sanctions
- Source: [IBAS — The world is now waiting for Kazakhstani chrysotile](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-the-world-is-waiting-for-kazakhstani-chrysotile.php), [IBAS — Kazakh Producers Chasing Russian Markets](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-kazakh-producers-chasing-russian-asbestos-markets.php)

## 5. Corporate Responsibility

### Kostanay Minerals JSC

- **Role:** Mine operator, processor, exporter — Kazakhstan's sole chrysotile asbestos producer.
- **Headquarters:** Zhitikara, Kostanay region, Kazakhstan.
- **Period of activity:** 1965–present.
- **Ownership:** Majority owned by Yerkin Tatishev through Kusto Group (see below).
- **Production:** ~250,000 tonnes/year (2021). Cumulative output: 17.5 million tonnes. 95–98% of production exported.
- **Workforce:** ~2,000 employees at the mine and processing complex; ~6,000 additional workers in downstream asbestos-cement companies.
- **Reserves:** Zhitikara deposit holds 37 million tonnes of chrysotile — 4th largest globally.
- **Health record:** Studies at the facility document occupational disease rates of 55.9 per 10,000 workers at the enrichment complex. 60% of examined workers showed pneumofibrosis. Workplace dust concentrations measured at 6 mg/m³ in 2014 — 12 times the national limit of 0.5 mg/m³. Workers with >25 years exposure show chromosomal abnormalities and immune system suppression.
- **Public messaging:** Company officials have publicly attacked ban advocates as "environmental extremists" (April 2022 rally). The company claims zero asbestos-disease diagnoses among workers (2018 conference) — contradicted by published peer-reviewed studies documenting pneumofibrosis, chronic bronchitis, and immune suppression.
- **Sources:** [IBAS — What is going on in Kazakhstan?](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-what-is-going-on-in-kazakhstan.php), [IBAS — The world is now waiting for Kazakhstani chrysotile](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-the-world-is-waiting-for-kazakhstani-chrysotile.php), [PMC — Human Exposure](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11734637/), [PMC — Immune Status](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9655464/)

### Kusto Group

- **Role:** Holding company; majority shareholder of Kostanay Minerals JSC.
- **Headquarters:** Singapore (originally Kazakhstan).
- **Founded by:** Yerkin Tatishev, one of Kazakhstan's richest businessmen (fortune estimated at $335 million).
- **Period of activity:** 1998–present. Tatishev served as Chairman of Kostanay Minerals JSC (1998–2004) and Orenburg Minerals JSC (Russia's largest asbestos producer) concurrently.
- **Operations:** 30+ companies in 9 countries (Vietnam, China, Thailand, Russia, Kazakhstan, Ukraine, Turkey, Georgia, Israel).
- **Corporate espionage ("Project Spring"):** In 2012, Kusto Group's managing director Daniel Kunin engaged K2 Intelligence (New York-based corporate intelligence firm) to infiltrate the global anti-asbestos movement. Operative **Robert Moore** posed as an investigative journalist and documentary filmmaker from 2012–2016, attending conferences in Washington, Geneva, Vietnam, and Thailand. He established a fake charity ("Stop Asbestos"), built relationships with movement leaders, and filed secret intelligence reports on activists including IBAS coordinator Laurie Kazan-Allen, Australian union leaders Barry Robson and Andrew Dettmer, asbestos expert Dr. Barry Castleman, and ADAO founder Linda Reinstein. Moore also surveilled WHO officials. Total cost: ~£500,000 in wages and expenses. Exposed in September 2016; legal proceedings followed. In November 2018, a confidential settlement with "substantial damages" was reached. UK courts granted Kusto Group an anonymity order. Over 35,000 confidential documents were returned.
- **Sources:** [KIAR — Lethal Lies](https://kiar.center/lethal-lies-how-a-corporate-spy-for-a-kazakhstan-company-infiltrated-the-global-anti-asbestos-network/), [IBAS — Corporate Deceit](https://ibasecretariat.org/lka-corporate-deceit-asbestos-espionage-at-home-and-abroad.php), [Wikipedia — Yerkin Tatishev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yerkin_Tatishev)

### International Chrysotile Association (ICA)

- **Role:** Industry lobby organization promoting "controlled use" of chrysotile asbestos globally.
- **Funding:** Asbestos mining companies in Russia and Kazakhstan, plus asbestos sellers in India and Mexico. ICA records show mines in Russia, Kazakhstan, and Brazil agreed to pay a "special contribution" for industry-funded research.
- **Activities:** Lobbies to defeat national asbestos bans, opposes chrysotile listing in the Rotterdam Convention, promotes "safe use" messaging in developing countries, funds research claiming chrysotile safety.
- **Kazakhstan link:** Kostanay Minerals and the Kazakh government actively participate in ICA activities. The company organizes "International Chrysotile Day" rallies (April 16) and sends delegations to Rotterdam Convention meetings.
- **Sources:** [RightOnCanada — Exposé of the ICA](https://rightoncanada.ca/?p=3917), [GBAN — Exposé](https://gban.net/2013/02/19/expose-of-the-international-chrysotile-association/), [PRNewswire — Kazakhstan Trade Union Conference](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/kazakhstan-hosted-international-trade-union-conference-on-chrysotile-asbestos-823447411.html)

### Context

Kazakhstan's official mesothelioma statistics are strikingly low — 17 cases in five years for a country that has mined chrysotile for six decades and where 50% of homes have asbestos roofing. The reported incidence of 0.28 per 100,000 is far below what epidemiological models would predict for a major producing country. Only 7.5% of diagnosed mesothelioma cases had documented occupational exposure history.

This dramatic undercount is attributed to multiple factors: the absence of a mesothelioma registry, inadequate diagnostic protocols, insufficient health surveillance of workers, and — critically — the political and economic incentive to suppress disease reporting in a country whose asbestos industry employs ~8,000 people and generates significant export revenue.

The BMC Public Health study (2022) comparing Brazil, China, Kazakhstan, and Russia found that tracheal, bronchus, and lung cancer was the leading cause of death and disability-adjusted life years (DALYs) attributable to asbestos in Kazakhstan between 1990 and 2019, followed by mesothelioma. While the age-standardized mortality rate declined by ~49% between 1990 and 2019, this likely reflects improved treatment rather than reduced exposure — production actually increased over this period.

The radiological study of 47 Kostanay Minerals workers (2008) is particularly damning: 60% showed pneumofibrosis in median lung zones, with chronic bronchitis in 6 workers and respiratory obstruction in 25%. Meanwhile, the company claimed at a 2018 conference that there were zero asbestos-disease diagnoses among workers.

The immunological study (Koigeldinova et al., 2022) at Kostanay Minerals documented significant immune suppression in workers with >15 years of exposure: decreased CD3+ and CD4+ T-cells, increased CD8+ T-cells, and elevated neutrophil phagocytic activity — consistent with chronic asbestos exposure effects.

## 7. Current Status (What's Happening NOW?)

### Is any ban being considered?

**No.** Kazakhstan shows no signs of considering an asbestos ban. The government actively promotes the chrysotile industry and has led international efforts to block regulation.

### Production trends

Production has been increasing. Kostanay Minerals targeted 350,000 tonnes/year by 2024 through additional work shifts (per 2021 statements by Chairman Erbol Nurkhozhaev). Q1 2023 production was 66,500 tonnes, up 21.9% from Q1 2022. In July 2025, Kostanay Minerals will celebrate 60 years of continuous chrysotile production.
Source: [IBAS News Archive](https://ibasecretariat.org/abs_archive_news.php?sel=c&c_val=Kazakhstan)

### Post-2022 market expansion

Russia's invasion of Ukraine disrupted asbestos logistics — Russian ports (Novorossiysk, St. Petersburg) became unavailable for shipments. This created an opportunity for Kazakhstan: Kostanay Minerals developed new export routes through Georgian ports (Poti, Batumi) and a China-Laos-Thailand rail link, reducing delivery times from 20 to 8 days. As IBAS noted, "Russian chrysotile has disappeared in most markets," and Kazakh producers are actively capturing Russian market share.
Source: [IBAS — Kazakh Producers Chasing Russian Markets](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-kazakh-producers-chasing-russian-asbestos-markets.php)

### Domestic consumption push

In October 2024, Kazakhstan opened its first asbestos-cement façade panel manufacturing facility, aiming to increase domestic consumption (currently only 7,000–15,000 tonnes/year vs. 220,000+ tonnes produced). A 2024 failed Alatau municipality development project included three asbestos-cement initiatives scheduled to create 284 jobs.
Source: [IBAS News Archive — Oct 2024](https://ibasecretariat.org/abs_archive_news.php?sel=c&c_val=Kazakhstan)

### Environmental health crisis in Zhitikara

In October 2024, Zhitikara residents mobilized to demand recognition as an environmental disaster zone due to elevated cancer incidence. The Ministry of Ecology issued inspection orders. This is the first significant grassroots challenge to the asbestos industry from within a mining community. Outcome of inspections not yet public as of early 2026.
Source: [IBAS News Archive — Oct 2024](https://ibasecretariat.org/abs_archive_news.php?sel=c&c_val=Kazakhstan)

### Rotterdam Convention

Kazakhstan remains part of the blocking coalition preventing chrysotile listing in the Rotterdam Convention (most recently at COP11, May 2023). Six countries — Kazakhstan, Russia, India, Kyrgyzstan, Zimbabwe, and Pakistan — overrode 159 parties supporting the listing. The next COP is expected in 2025.
Source: [Wikipedia — Rotterdam Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotterdam_Convention)

## Verification Notes

### Data gaps that need human follow-up:

1. **"2019 ban" claim:** One secondary source claims a 2019 ban effective January 2021. All primary sources (IBAS, USGS, Kostanay Minerals' own production data) show this is false — production continued and increased. However, a human researcher should attempt to identify what legislation, if any, this claim refers to. It may be a confusion with another country or a misinterpretation of the 2013 hazardous waste classification.

2. **Specific sanitary regulation number:** Kazakhstan's MPC limits for asbestos dust (0.5 mg/m³ / 1 mg/m³) are referenced in multiple studies but the specific regulation number and text could not be located in English-language sources.

3. **Mesothelioma undercount magnitude:** The reported 17 cases over 5 years is almost certainly a dramatic undercount. The BMC study predicts 6,500 cases over 15 years (~433/year), which is orders of magnitude higher than reported. This gap deserves investigation — ideally through WHO country office contacts.

4. **GreenWomen current status:** The NGO was active in 2009–2011. Their current operational status and any ongoing asbestos work could not be confirmed from available sources.

5. **Kostanay Minerals website:** The company profile page (km.kz/en/company-profile/) returned a 404 error. The about page (km.kz/en/about-company/) may have different content — could not verify.

6. **Kusto Group anonymity order:** UK courts granted Kusto Group anonymity in the Project Spring espionage case. The KIAR article and IBAS reporting identified them, but the UK court order may still be in effect.

7. **2024 Ministry of Ecology inspections:** Results of the inspections ordered in October 2024 following Zhitikara residents' mobilization have not been publicly reported as of April 2026.

8. **Autopsy study details:** The autopsy study of 343 deceased (finding dust particles in 44.6% of non-exposed residents) is referenced in the PMC review article but the primary study citation (authors, year, journal) was not extracted. This needs verification.

### Contradictions found:

- **Founding year of Kostanay Minerals:** IBAS and most sources cite 1965. One page on the company's own website (now 404) cited 1916, which likely refers to the discovery or early small-scale mining of the deposit, not the establishment of the current enterprise.
- **2023 production:** IBAS cites 260,000 tonnes; Statista (citing USGS) cites 210,000 tonnes. The discrepancy may reflect different measurement methodologies (crude ore vs. processed fiber) or different reporting periods.
- **Worker health claims vs. evidence:** Kostanay Minerals claimed "zero asbestos-disease diagnoses" at a 2018 conference. Published peer-reviewed studies at the same facility document pneumofibrosis in 60% of examined workers, chronic bronchitis, immune suppression, and chromosomal abnormalities. This is a direct contradiction.

## Suggested Improvements

1. **Contact GreenWomen directly** — the Kazakh NGO that co-organized the 2009 Astana conference may have unpublished data, local contacts, and insight into whether any domestic advocacy work continues.
2. **Access WHO Mortality Database for Kazakhstan** — the reported mesothelioma figures (0.28/100,000) are almost certainly underreported. WHO data may reveal more complete mortality figures, or at minimum document the data gap officially.
3. **Research Kazakh-language sources** — this profile relies heavily on English-language sources. Kazakh and Russian-language news, legislative databases, and health registries likely contain additional regulatory details, health data, and activism stories not accessible in English.
4. **FOI request to Ministry of Ecology** — request results of the October 2024 Zhitikara facility inspections.
5. **Track Rotterdam Convention COP12** — expected in 2025. Kazakhstan's position on chrysotile listing will be a key indicator of any policy shift.
6. **Interview Zhitikara residents** — the 2024 mobilization represents a rare window for first-person accounts from a mining community. Given the history of corporate espionage, any outreach must be conducted with sensitivity to personal safety.
7. **Verify 2013 hazardous waste classification** — locate the specific regulation and its scope. This may be the basis for the false "2019 ban" claim (misinterpretation or date confusion).
8. **Research Dr. Lavrenty Aizvert** — Kazakhstan Ministry of Health toxicologist who in 2021 publicly dismissed asbestos health risks. His role and influence on government policy warrants further investigation.
