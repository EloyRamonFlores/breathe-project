## 2. Historias de Resistencia

### Ana Cecilia Niño Robles (2014–2017)
Ana Cecilia Niño was a journalist who grew up in the Pablo Neruda district of Sibaté — a neighborhood close to Colombia's largest asbestos-cement factory. She never worked with asbestos. In September 2014, a year after giving birth to her first child, she was diagnosed with mesothelioma. With her husband Daniel Pineda, she co-founded "Colombia sin Asbesto" (Colombia Without Asbestos) and launched a national media campaign that combined journalism, social media, community education at schools and universities, and direct political lobbying — all while undergoing treatment. In 2016, the Senate Committee rejected the ban; Ana and Daniel responded by filing a complaint at the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights and collecting over 50,000 signatures. On January 8, 2017, Ana died at age 42. In October 2017, the Senate Committee approved the bill. President Duque signed it into law in July 2019. The law bears her name.

**Quote:** "They removed a lung, my pericardium, and my left internal muscles, but they will never take away my hope of seeing a Colombia without asbestos."
**Quote source:** 2016 social media post, documented in Confront Power investigative report (https://confrontpower.org/colombia-asbestos-campaign/)

**Source:** [Confront Power: The family whose campaign defeated the Colombian asbestos industry](https://confrontpower.org/colombia-asbestos-campaign/)

### Colombia sin Asbesto / Ban Asbestos Colombia (2014–present)
The coalition "Colombia sin Asbesto" — founded by Ana Cecilia and Daniel Pineda in 2014 — brought together asbestos victims, families, scientists, and public health advocates to push for national legislation. The coalition documented Sibaté's environmental contamination, built connections with the IBAS international network, and maintained political pressure through three years of parliamentary rejection, a lawsuit at the Inter-American Commission on Human Rights, and a 50,000-signature petition. The campaign's success in securing a named law — the "Ana Cecilia Niño Law" — represents a landmark in Latin American public health advocacy.

## 3. Exposure Sources

### Sibaté — Environmental Contamination
Sibaté (population ~40,000, 25 km southwest of Bogotá) is the most severely asbestos-contaminated municipality in Colombia. The Eternit Colombia factory operated from 1942 until 2002. The company discharged untreated wastewater into the El Muña reservoir until 1999, and illegal wastewater discharges were also identified in 2015. A 2019 environmental study found asbestos contamination in approximately one-third of the town's subsoil — near a school, apartment buildings, a football stadium, and a sports center. Sibaté has the highest age-adjusted mesothelioma mortality rate in Colombia: 38.36 per 100,000 — far exceeding Bogotá (1.89 per 100,000). [PubMed: Sibaté case study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/31229775/)

### Industrial Manufacturing — Bogotá/Cundinamarca Region
Beyond the Sibaté factory, asbestos-cement products were distributed across Colombia's construction sector. Asbestos-cement roofing sheets, pipes, water tanks, and tiles were used in residential, commercial, and public infrastructure throughout the country. GIS studies estimate significant populations in the Bogotá metropolitan area remain at risk from legacy materials. [PMC: GIS estimate Colombia](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8703708/)

### Friction Products
Brake linings and friction materials were a secondary exposure pathway for automotive workers and mechanics throughout the country. [IBAS: Asbestos in Colombia 2012](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-asbestos-in-colombia-2012.php)

## 4. Corporate Responsibility

### Eternit Colombia S.A.
Eternit Colombia operated the Sibaté asbestos-cement plant from 1942 to 2002. The factory discharged asbestos-contaminated waste across the town — contaminating subsoil, a reservoir, school grounds, sports facilities, and residential areas. Despite the connection between Sibaté's contamination and Eternit's waste disposal practices being documented in peer-reviewed research, Eternit Colombia has not been subject to a criminal conviction equivalent to the Italian Eternit trial. Ana Cecilia Niño's case — environmental (not occupational) exposure, from growing up in the shadow of the Sibaté plant — became the symbolic center of the ban campaign. [Confront Power: Colombia campaign](https://confrontpower.org/colombia-asbestos-campaign/); [IBAS: Eternit's Global Asbestos Crimes](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-eternits-global-asbestos-crimes.php)

## 6. Current Status

### Ban in Force, Enforcement Weak
Law 1968 of 2019 (the "Ana Cecilia Niño Law") took effect January 1, 2021. All exploitation, production, commercialization, use, import, distribution, and export of any variety of asbestos is prohibited. Violations carry fines of 100 to 5,000 minimum legal monthly wages. However, as of 2024–2025, the law's implementation infrastructure remains incomplete:

- The National Commission for Asbestos Substitution (CNSA) was required to be operational by January 1, 2021, but the definitive applicant list was only published in April 2024, with formal observer notification set for August 2025.
- The required public asbestos awareness campaign has not been launched by the government.
- Air quality monitoring of asbestos fiber concentrations in affected areas has not commenced.
- Worker health surveillance programs remain delayed.
- No asbestos replacement public policy was designed by the 2024 deadline as required by the law.

[IBAS: Colombia ban in name but not in fact](https://ibasecretariat.org/mtt-colombia-has-a-ban-asbestos-law-in-name-but-not-in-fact.php); [MinAmbiente: Sustitución del asbesto](https://www.minambiente.gov.co/sustitucion-del-asbesto/)

### 2024 Bogotá Conference
A national conference titled "Asbestos in Colombia 2024: Challenges After the Ban" was held in Bogotá to review implementation progress of the Ana Cecilia Niño Law, discuss management of legacy asbestos in buildings, and assess surveillance of exposed populations. This represents civil society and academic pressure on government to deliver the law's promised programs.

### Legacy Buildings
Like Brazil, Colombia has no national removal or registry program for the millions of buildings with legacy asbestos-cement materials. The law exempts "asbestos installed before January 1, 2021" from the prohibition — meaning legacy materials remain in situ indefinitely without a mandated remediation program. [Brigard Urrutia: 2021 asbestos prohibition](https://www.bu.com.co/en/insights/noticias/2021-use-asbestos-will-be-prohibited-colombia)

## Verification Notes

- **Ana Cecilia Niño birth year**: The plan notes her possible birth year as 1974 or 1975. Source says she died January 8, 2017 at age 42. If she died at age 42 in January 2017, she was born in 1974 (if birthday is after Jan 8) or 1975 (if before Jan 8). The ADAO memorial says age 42; Semana says the same. Birth year is not precisely stated in any accessible source — do not include in countries.json to avoid guessing.
- **Ana Cecilia quote**: "They removed a lung, my pericardium, and my left internal muscles, but they will never take away my hope of seeing a Colombia without asbestos" — from her 2016 social media post, documented in the Confront Power article which is investigative journalism (not a verbatim transcript). Treating as verified quote (sourced to a published article describing the specific post).
- **"Four women" reference**: The ADAO and mesothelioma.net sources mention "the activism of four women" played a major role; the Confront Power article focuses on Ana Cecilia and Daniel Pineda. The three other women are not individually named in sources accessed. Did not include uncorroborated names.
- **Law 1968 "in name but not in fact" (IBAS 2021)**: This is the primary source for enforcement failures. As of 2024, some progress has been made (CNSA applicant list published April 2024), but full implementation remains incomplete. The critique remains valid.
- **Mesothelioma rate for countries.json**: PMC 2025 study gives 0.59 per 100,000 as peak (2017) and ~0.4785 average for 2013–2022. Setting `mesothelioma_rate: 0.5` (approximate, from 2022 data point) would be misleading given underdiagnosis issues. Recommend flagging as null in JSON and noting this in the entry.
