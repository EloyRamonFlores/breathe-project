## 2. Historias de Resistencia

### Richard Spoor (1990s–present)
Richard Spoor is a South African human rights attorney based in White River, Mpumalanga, who over more than 25 years built landmark cases for communities devastated by asbestos and silicosis. In the late 1990s and early 2000s, Spoor represented thousands of mine workers and their families from the Northern Cape and Limpopo Province against Gencor, GEFCO, and Swiss multinational Becon AG (owner of Kuruman Cape Blue Asbestos and Danielskuil Cape Blue Asbestos). His work resulted in two trust funds: the Asbestos Relief Trust (funded by a 2003 Gencor settlement) and the Kgalagadi Relief Trust (funded by a 2006 Becon settlement). Together these trusts have paid more than R540 million in compensation to over 6,000 claimants, with the ART operating as an open settlement until 2028 to account for asbestos diseases' long latency periods.

### Schalk Willem Burger Lubbe — and the Test-Case Claimants (1997–2003)
Mrs. Lubbe (continued after her death by her husband Schalk Willem Burger Lubbe) was among five South African plaintiffs who filed the first English High Court claim against Cape plc in February 1997. The case established a precedent that has shaped international corporate accountability law: on July 20, 2000, the UK House of Lords ruled unanimously that English courts could hear claims from foreign plaintiffs injured by the overseas operations of British companies. By 2001, approximately 7,500 claimants had joined. A 2003 settlement saw Cape pay £7.5 million and Gencor establish a £35 million trust fund for South African victims. The Lubbe ruling is now cited globally to hold parent companies accountable for the conduct of subsidiaries in lower-income countries.

### Asbestos Interest Group (AIG), Kuruman (2006–present)
The Asbestos Interest Group is a citizens' organization based in Kuruman, Northern Cape, that emerged from communities living amid the 82+ documented asbestos mine dumps in the region. AIG documented contamination at schools including Gamopedi Primary, Reitemogetse Primary, Sedibeng Primary, and Khiba Middle School — contamination that authorities initially ignored. AIG's advocacy contributed directly to the 2014 closure of Khiba Middle School and to the international solidarity letters from the UK Parliamentary Group on Occupational Safety and Health in 2015. Their work illustrates the continued struggle of Griqua and Coloured communities in the former crocidolite mining zone to access both remediation and healthcare.

## 3. Exposure Sources

### Three Fiber Types, Three Regions
South Africa is unique globally in having commercially mined all three principal asbestos fiber types in distinct geographic regions:

- **Crocidolite (blue asbestos)** — Northern Cape Province: the "Asbestos Mountains" belt running from Prieska/Koegas through Kuruman to Griquatown and Daniëlskuil. Companies: Cape Asbestos Company (later GEFCO/Gencor), Kuruman Cape Blue Asbestos, Danielskuil Cape Blue Asbestos (Becon AG). The Koegas mine, opened by Cape Asbestos Company in 1893 near Prieska, became one of the world's largest crocidolite operations. South Africa supplied approximately 97% of the world's crocidolite. [IBAS SA Profile](https://ibasecretariat.org/prof_sa.php)

- **Amosite (brown asbestos)** — Limpopo Province (formerly northeastern Transvaal): Pietersburg (now Polokwane) fields; Penge mine. Companies: Gefco, Gencor. South Africa supplied virtually 100% of the world's commercially mined amosite. [Social History Portal](https://socialhistoryportal.org/news/articles/109725)

- **Chrysotile (white asbestos)** — Mpumalanga Province: Barberton area (Msauli and Hanova mines). Minor compared to other fiber types. Companies: African Chrysotile Asbestos, Msauli, Hanova Mining. [Asbestos Relief Trust](https://asbestostrust.co.za/asbestos-relief-trust/)

### Environmental Contamination
- Approximately 82+ asbestos dumps documented in the Northern Cape alone as of the late 1990s [IBAS SA Legacy](https://ibasecretariat.org/lka_sa_leg.php)
- Rivers (including the Orange/Gariep) contaminated by mine tailings runoff — residents gathered wood and children swam in contaminated water [PMC: Social Production](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1522094/)
- Asbestos-cement roofing sheets used to build homes in mining communities using contaminated material from mine waste
- Entire school grounds in Kuruman area built on asbestos-contaminated land [IBAS: SA Asbestos Crisis](https://ibasecretariat.org/lka-south-africas-asbestos-crisis.php)

### Racial Dimension of Exposure
The social structure of South African apartheid-era mining shaped who was most exposed:
- Black workers were legally excluded from skilled positions reserved for white employees; wage differentials reached 10-fold for identical work in the 1970s
- Women and children were integral to production as unregistered and unprotected laborers
- Communities in the Northern Cape were predominantly Griqua and Coloured South Africans — among the most economically marginalized groups in the country
- Exposure levels in the late 1970s were documented at 10 times higher than comparable European operations [PMC: Social Production](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1522094/)
- Environmental exposure (not occupational) accounts for 30% of mesothelioma cases — predominantly affecting women and children who never worked in mines [asbestos.com: South Africa](https://www.asbestos.com/mesothelioma/south-africa/)

### Construction and Industrial Uses
- Asbestos-cement roofing and cladding (Everite-Eternit products, widely used in low-cost housing)
- Pipe insulation in mines, railways, and industrial facilities
- Textiles, brake linings, gaskets (Turner & Newall products)
- Boilermaking, shipbuilding, railway maintenance workers across major cities

## 4. Corporate Responsibility

### Cape Asbestos Company / Cape plc (UK)
Cape Asbestos Company opened the Koegas mine near Prieska in 1893. By the 1970s, internal company documents showed Cape was aware of the hazards. In 1997, Mrs. Lubbe and four South Africans filed the first English court claim alleging Cape exposed workers to 30 times the British legal limit of asbestos dust without protective gear. Cape attempted forum non conveniens — claiming the case should be heard in South Africa (where legal aid was unavailable). The House of Lords rejected this on July 20, 2000. In the 2003 settlement, Cape paid £7.5 million to approximately 7,500 claimants represented by Leigh Day. [IBAS: House of Lords Victory](https://ibasecretariat.org/lka_lords.php); [Business & Human Rights Centre: Cape/Gencor](https://www.business-humanrights.org/en/latest-news/capegencor-lawsuits-re-so-africa/)

### Gencor / GEFCO (South Africa)
GEFCO (Griqualand Exploration and Finance Company) acquired Cape's Northern Cape mines in 1980 and operated until 1996. Gencor was the corporate successor. In 2003, Gencor established and funded the £35 million Asbestos Relief Trust, which operates as an open settlement until 2028. The ART has paid R416 million to 4,700 claimants as of early 2024. [ART website](https://asbestostrust.co.za/asbestos-relief-trust/)

### Becon AG (Switzerland)
Becon AG, a Swiss company, owned Kuruman Cape Blue Asbestos and Danielskuil Cape Blue Asbestos. In 2006, Richard Spoor negotiated the Becon settlement, establishing the Kgalagadi Relief Trust (KRT). The KRT has paid R123 million to 1,582 claimants as of February 2024. [KRT website](https://asbestostrust.co.za/krt/)

### Turner & Newall (UK)
Operated mines and processing facilities in Limpopo and left unrehabilitated asbestos dumps in the former Lebowa homeland area. Documented in academic literature for leaving contaminated waste. [PMC: Social Production](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1522094/)

## 6. Current Status

### Contamination Persists
The Northern Cape and Limpopo remain severely contaminated. At least 82 documented asbestos mine dumps exist in the Northern Cape alone. The government's National Asbestos Management Strategy targets "asbestos risk-free status by 2030," but the parliamentary briefing (PMG.org.za) noted significant challenges: insufficient funding and poor coordination between government departments. [PMG: Asbestos Roadmap](https://pmg.org.za/committee-meeting/28882/)

### 2020 Abatement Regulations
The Asbestos Abatement Regulations 2020 established mandatory requirements for the identification, assessment, and removal of asbestos-containing materials in workplaces and properties. In July 2024, amendments strengthened restrictions on power tools and prohibited new fittings onto ACMs. Compliance timelines have been contested by property owners. [Lexology: Asbestos Abatement SA](https://www.lexology.com/library/detail.aspx?g=668a09fe-9a05-4ff9-a246-0623c55037f9)

### Schools Crisis
As of 2014–2015, multiple schools in the Kuruman area remained contaminated. Khiba Middle School (200 students) was closed in October 2014 — eight years after the contamination was first documented by the Department of Environmental Affairs in 2006. Community advocacy by AIG and international attention from the UK Parliamentary Group pressed for action. [IBAS: SA Asbestos Crisis](https://ibasecretariat.org/lka-south-africas-asbestos-crisis.php)

### Trust Funds Ongoing
Both the Asbestos Relief Trust and the Kgalagadi Relief Trust continue to accept claims. The ART operates until 2028. As of February 2024: ART has paid R416 million to 4,700 claimants; KRT has paid R123 million to 1,582 claimants. [ART website](https://asbestostrust.co.za/asbestos-relief-trust/)
