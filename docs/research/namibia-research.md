### Context

Namibia's asbestos regulatory history is inseparable from its colonial past. As German South West Africa (1884–1915) and then under South African administration (1915–1990), Namibia inherited regulatory frameworks and construction practices from its colonial powers. The 1969 Windhoek Building Regulations explicitly recognized asbestos-cement sheets as standard roofing material — reflecting the era's acceptance of asbestos in construction across southern Africa.

After independence in 1990, Namibia developed its own regulatory framework. The 1997 Health and Safety Regulations include specific "Asbestos Regulations" under Schedule 2, but the detailed content (exposure limits, prohibited fiber types, handling requirements) could not be verified from publicly accessible digital sources. The regulations appear to focus on occupational exposure management rather than a product ban.

Critically, Namibia is NOT on the IBAS list of countries with national asbestos bans. No evidence was found of a comprehensive ban on any asbestos fiber type. The 2024 Draft OSH Bill proposes modernizing the occupational health framework but does not appear to include specific asbestos ban provisions.

## 2. Historias de Resistencia (Activism Stories)

### Individual Stories

*No Namibia-specific asbestos activists or advocacy movements were identified in public sources.* This reflects several factors:

1. Namibia was not a major asbestos producer (unlike South Africa) — exposure was primarily through imported products and migrant labor
2. Occupational health advocacy in the mining sector has focused on uranium (Rössing mine) rather than asbestos
3. Namibian migrant workers who were exposed in South African asbestos mines returned home with diseases but without organized advocacy structures

The closest relevant activism involves the **Kgalagadi Relief Trust (KRT)** and **Asbestos Relief Trust (ART)** — established through litigation against South African mining companies — which provide compensation to former mine workers and environmental exposure victims from the Northern Cape region. However, access is restricted to employees of participating mines, creating barriers for Namibian migrant workers who may have been exposed.

- **Sources:** [PMC1522094](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1522094/), [PMC3556709](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3556709/)

## 4. Exposure Sources ("Where Was It?")

### Common asbestos-containing materials in Namibia

Namibia did not have significant domestic asbestos manufacturing. Asbestos-containing materials were predominantly imported from South Africa, which was a major global producer of all three commercially mined asbestos types (chrysotile, crocidolite, amosite).

### Key locations of exposure

- **Mines:** Namibia had no major asbestos mines. However, crocidolite (blue asbestos) deposits exist in the Kunene Region near Outjo, associated with tiger's eye and pietersite mineral formations. These were mined primarily for semi-precious stones, not asbestos fiber.
  - The primary mining-related exposure came from **Namibian migrant workers in South African Northern Cape crocidolite mines.** Workers migrated from Namibia to mines near Prieska and Kuruman along a 240-mile crocidolite belt extending to the Botswana border. They lived in mine hostels during contract periods and returned home when contracts expired. Their disease experiences were systematically excluded from South African statistics.
  - Source: [PMC1522094](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1522094/), [Stonemania](https://www.stonemania.co.uk/crystals/p/pietersite)

- **Factories:** No asbestos-cement factories confirmed in Namibia. Modern cement production (Ohorongo Cement, established ~2010 near Otavi) does not use asbestos. Building materials were historically imported from South Africa.
  - Source: [EIB](https://www.eib.org/en/infocentre/stories/all/2014-june-01/namibias-only-cement-factory-lives-up-to-its-promise.htm)

- **Products:** Asbestos-cement roofing sheets (corrugated and flat), pipes, insulation — all imported from South Africa where Eternit and other producers operated. German colonial-era architecture (1884–1915) predates widespread asbestos-cement use, but post-WWII South African-administered construction (1950s–1980s) incorporated asbestos-cement products extensively.
  - Source: [Windhoek CC Building Regulations](https://www.windhoekcc.org.na/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/Windhoek-Building-Regulations.pdf)

- **Buildings:** The 1969 Windhoek Building Regulations explicitly list asbestos-cement sheets alongside galvanised steel and aluminium as standard "light roof construction" materials. Buildings constructed in Windhoek, Walvis Bay, Swakopmund, and other urban centers during the 1950s–1980s South African administration period are most likely to contain asbestos-cement roofing and water piping.
  - Source: [Windhoek CC Building Regulations](https://www.windhoekcc.org.na/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/Windhoek-Building-Regulations.pdf)

- **Uranium mining (indirect):** Occupational health assessments at Arandis (near Rössing uranium mine) have included asbestos surveys, suggesting awareness of asbestos presence in mining infrastructure even where asbestos is not the primary mineral extracted.
  - Source: [CoHygienist Projects](http://cohygienist.co.za/Pages/Projects.asp)

## 4. Corporate Responsibility

### South African Mining Companies (cross-border exposure)

Namibian migrant workers were exposed in South African Northern Cape mines operated by companies including those that later formed the basis of asbestos litigation:

- **Cape PLC / Gencor / Turner & Newall:** These companies operated crocidolite and amosite mines in the Northern Cape where migrant workers from Namibia, Malawi, Mozambique, Lesotho, Swaziland, and Botswana were employed. Specific Namibian worker numbers are not available in public sources.
- **Compensation access:** The Kgalagadi Relief Trust (KRT) and Asbestos Relief Trust (ART) were established through litigation settlements. Access is restricted to past employees of participating mines and nearby environmental exposure victims. Cross-border workers face significant barriers: they must navigate multiple jurisdictions, often lack documentation, and their disease experiences were excluded from South African statistics.
- **Source:** [PMC1522094](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC1522094/), [PMC3556709](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3556709/)

*Note: No Namibia-based companies were identified as major asbestos producers, importers, or manufacturers. Exposure in Namibia came through imported South African building materials and through migrant labor in South African mines.*

### Context

Namibia's asbestos mortality data is essentially nonexistent in international databases. This reflects several compounding factors:

1. **Migrant labor invisibility:** Namibian workers exposed in South African mines returned home with diseases, but "their disease experiences were not included in the limited number of South African statistics" (McCulloch, 2005). Their diseases and deaths were recorded (if at all) in Namibia, where the occupational connection to South African mines was likely lost.

2. **No cancer registry with asbestos-specific tracking:** Unlike Taiwan or the UK, Namibia does not have a population-based cancer registry with mesothelioma-specific coding that would allow epidemiological analysis.

3. **Diagnostic capacity:** Mesothelioma is difficult to diagnose even in well-resourced healthcare systems. In Namibia's resource-constrained health system, mesothelioma cases are likely misdiagnosed or classified under broader categories.

4. **Environmental exposure unmeasured:** Buildings constructed with South African asbestos-cement products during 1950s–1980s continue to age and deteriorate, but no environmental exposure assessment has been conducted.

The true burden of asbestos-related disease in Namibia is unknown but is likely concentrated in: (a) former migrant mine workers who worked in South Africa's Northern Cape; (b) construction workers who handled imported asbestos-cement products; (c) residents of buildings with deteriorating asbestos-cement roofing.

## 6. Current Status (What's Happening NOW?)

- **Is there a ban being enforced?** NO confirmed ban exists. The 1997 Asbestos Regulations under the Labour Act provide occupational exposure management rules, but their enforcement status is unknown.

- **Ongoing removal programs?** No national asbestos removal or abatement program was identified. South Africa's 2020 Asbestos Abatement Regulations (which require property owners to develop management plans for asbestos-containing materials) do not extend to Namibia.
  - Source: [SA Asbestos Abatement Regulations 2020](https://www.labour.gov.za/DocumentCenter/Regulations%20and%20Notices/Regulations/Occupational%20Health%20and%20Safety/Asbestos%20Abatement%20Regulations,%202020%20GG43893.pdf)

- **Pending legislation?** The Draft Occupational Safety and Health Bill (May 2024) proposes to overhaul the current OSH framework. Key improvements include: mandatory safety committees (workplaces 20+ workers), inspector direct fines up to N$200,000, 45-year record-keeping for accidents, 30-year exposure records. However, no asbestos-specific ban or phase-out provisions have been confirmed in the draft.
  - Source: [ENSafrica](https://www.ensafrica.com/news/detail/10359/namibias-occupational-safety-and-health-legal), [MOL.gov.na Draft Bill](https://mol.gov.na/documents/53329/0/NAM+OSH+Bill+FIRST+DRAFT+2024+(1).pdf/14fe60f2-5069-1df5-1eda-d1e91c3f0166)

- **Active lawsuits?** No Namibia-specific asbestos lawsuits identified. Former Namibian mine workers may be eligible for compensation through South Africa's Kgalagadi Relief Trust and Asbestos Relief Trust, but cross-border access remains a documented challenge.

- **Recent news:** The focus of occupational health advocacy in Namibia's mining sector has been on uranium exposure (Rössing mine, Arandis) rather than asbestos. Workers at Rössing have been documented with elevated uranium excretion and chromosomal aberrations, and the LaRRI (Labour Resource and Research Institute) has raised concerns about worker health information access.
  - Source: [SOMO](https://www.somo.nl/uranium-workers-namibia-unaware-of-severe-health-risks/)

- **Building stock at risk:** No inventory or estimate exists for asbestos-containing buildings in Namibia. The 1969 building regulations confirm asbestos-cement was used, but the scale is unquantified.
