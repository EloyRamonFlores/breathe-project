## 2. Historias de Resistencia

### Fernanda Giannasi (1983–present)
Fernanda Giannasi is a Labour Inspector with Brazil's Ministry of Labor (since 1983) who became the leading national activist for an asbestos ban. She co-founded ABREA (Associação Brasileira dos Expostos ao Amianto) in 1995 and served as coordinator for Latin America of the Citizens' Virtual Network Against Asbestos. Giannasi documented conditions at the Eternit factory in Osasco, led enforcement actions against the asbestos industry, and supported 3,500 workers who filed lawsuits. The industry responded with death threats, criminal defamation charges filed by Eternit and Saint-Gobain, and a period of professional exile — she was assigned to a tiny office with no computer, telephone, or responsibilities. Canada's government also pressured Brazilian authorities against her work. Despite these attacks, Giannasi remained active through the 2017 STF ruling and the 2023 ban reaffirmation. Following the February 2023 STF ruling, she stated the court had "protected human beings from a deadly threat" while condemning continued exports as a "national disgrace."

**Quote:** "I defend an immediate world ban on the production, marketing and use of asbestos."
**Quote source:** PMC: Fernanda Giannasi — battling asbestos in Brazil, Journal of Epidemiology and Community Health, 2006 (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2465482/)

### ABREA — Associação Brasileira dos Expostos ao Amianto (1995–present)
Founded in 1995 by former workers of Eternit's Osasco factory, ABREA became Brazil's primary advocacy organization for asbestos victims. A 2003 ABREA survey of 1,022 former Eternit workers documented 24 deaths from asbestos-related disease, 223 respiratory disorder cases, 192 cases of pleural plaques, and 109 asbestosis cases. ABREA's documentation work built the evidentiary base for Brazil's national litigation against the industry and contributed directly to the STF constitutional challenges. The organization continues to provide support to sick workers and their families.

## 3. Exposure Sources

### Chrysotile Mining — Minaçu, Goiás
SAMA Minerações Associadas, an Eternit S.A. subsidiary, operated the Minaçu mine in Goiás state — Brazil's only chrysotile asbestos mine. At its peak, Brazil produced over 300,000 tonnes/year (2012), making it the world's third-largest chrysotile producer. Between 2002 and 2012, Brazil exported 61% of domestically produced asbestos. After the 2017 ban, SAMA obtained injunctions for export-only operations through a Goiás state law; despite the ban, SAMA reported a 246% increase in sales in June 2021 and revenue of US$96.6 million in 2022. [IBAS: Brazil Profile](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/prof_brazil.php); [IBAS: Closing Loopholes](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-closing-brazils-asbestos-loopholes.php)

### Industrial Manufacturing — Osasco and São Paulo State
The Eternit factory in Osasco (opened 1939, closed 1993) was the center of Brazil's asbestos-cement industry for 54 years. Workers poured asbestos into mixers with cement, emptied bags, and cleaned machinery immersed in fiber-rich white dust with no respiratory protection until 1977 when they received paper masks. Three generations of workers were exposed. Products included asbestos-cement roofing sheets (telhas), pipes, and water tanks — widely used in low-cost housing construction across Brazil. [Al Jazeera: Brazil asbestos victims](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2003/10/23/brazils-asbestos-victims-gasp-for-justice)

### Legacy Asbestos in Buildings
Brazil produced asbestos-cement products extensively from the 1960s to the 1990s. Telhas onduladas (corrugated asbestos-cement roofing sheets) are found in tens of millions of low-income homes, rural buildings, and public infrastructure. [BrazilianNR: Challenges 2024](https://braziliannr.com/2024/10/14/challenges-of-asbestos-in-brazil-ban-vigilance-and-safe-removal/)

### Friction Products
Brake linings, clutches, and other friction materials were a secondary major use category — relevant to automotive workers and mechanics across the country. [IBAS Brazil Profile](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/prof_brazil.php)

## 4. Corporate Responsibility

### Eternit S.A. (Brazil) / SAMA Minerações Associadas
Eternit S.A. is the Brazilian parent company that operated the Osasco factory (closed 1993) and owns SAMA Minerações Associadas (Minaçu mine). In 2013, Eternit was ordered by a Brazilian court to pay healthcare costs for asbestos victims. In 2014 it faced a class action. On June 9, 2021, a São Paulo Court convicted SAMA, Eternit SA, and a transport company for violating state and federal asbestos laws. Despite the 2017 national ban, Eternit's 2022 revenue from asbestos exports reached US$96.6 million — a 55% increase from 2021 — through a loophole created by Goiás state legislation. [IBAS: Closing Loopholes](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-closing-brazils-asbestos-loopholes.php); [mesothelioma.com: Brazil continues mining](https://www.mesothelioma.com/blog/brazil-continues-to-mine-and-export-asbestos-despite-ban/)

### Canadian Government Pressure
Canada (then a major chrysotile exporter) pressured Brazilian authorities against Fernanda Giannasi's regulatory enforcement work in the early 2000s — a documented example of foreign industry influence on another country's domestic asbestos policy. [PMC: Fernanda Giannasi](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2465482/)

### Saint-Gobain (France)
Supporters of Saint-Gobain France filed criminal defamation charges against Fernanda Giannasi in connection with her enforcement and advocacy work. [PMC: Fernanda Giannasi](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC2465482/)

## 6. Current Status

### Ban Technically in Force, Enforcement Contested
The November 29, 2017 STF ruling (7-2, ADI 3937/3406/3470) banned all chrysotile mining, processing, marketing, and distribution, declaring Federal Law 9.055/95 unconstitutional. The February 23, 2023 ruling (7-1) reaffirmed this. However, as of late 2024, the STF has not resolved ADI 6200, which challenges the Goiás state law allowing export operations. A scheduled August 14, 2024 vote was interrupted and postponed indefinitely. The SAMA/Eternit mine in Minaçu operated profitably for years post-ban via injunctions and the Goiás law loophole. [IBAS: Brazil Asbestos Ban Upheld](https://www.ibasecretariat.org/lka-brazil-asbestos-ban-upheld.php); [ADAO: Brazil STF postponement](https://www.asbestosdiseaseawareness.org/newsroom/blogs/brazil-federal-supreme-court-upholds-decision-to-ban-asbestos-mining-processing-and-export/)

### Goiás Five-Year Phase-Out (2023)
In August 2023, the state of Goiás enacted Law No. 22,935 establishing a five-year deadline for all asbestos activities. This represents a state-level acknowledgment that the industry must eventually close. The rare-earth transition in Minaçu began in 2024 (American-funded operation), but early shipments to China are far below production targets. [BrazilianNR: Challenges 2024](https://braziliannr.com/2024/10/14/challenges-of-asbestos-in-brazil-ban-vigilance-and-safe-removal/)

### Legacy Buildings — Major Unresolved Issue
Brazil's ban does not address the millions of buildings with asbestos-cement roofing. Low-income communities face ongoing exposure from deteriorating telhas. There is no national removal or registry program comparable to France's DTA. [BrazilianNR: Legacy Asbestos 2024](https://braziliannr.com/2024/08/10/a-call-for-the-total-ban-on-asbestos-in-brazil/)

## Verification Notes

- **Fernanda Giannasi quote**: "I defend an immediate world ban on the production, marketing and use of asbestos" — sourced from PMC article (2006) which cites the original *Journal of Epidemiology and Community Health* paper. This is a published, verified verbatim quote.
- **STF ruling numbers**: ADI 3937 (São Paulo state law constitutionality), ADI 3406 and ADI 3470 (federal law unconstitutionality) — all November 29, 2017. February 2023 ruling is the final judgment on appeals in ADI 3937. ADI 6200 is the separate pending challenge to Goiás state export law.
- **"Law 9055/90 vs 9055/95"**: Sources use both citations. The IBAS ban article uses "9.055/90" while other sources use "9.055/1995." This appears to be inconsistent citation of the same law; "9.055/1995" is the correct IBAS-preferred citation — used throughout this profile.
- **Minaçu mine status 2025**: Inside Climate News (July 2025) reports rare-earth transition in Minaçu with only 479 total tons shipped to China by early 2025, far below 5,000-ton annual target. Asbestos mining status under Goiás phase-out law appears to be in wind-down, pending STF ADI 6200 resolution.
- **Mesothelioma rate in countries.json**: Existing entry has `mesothelioma_rate: null`. The 2017 male rate of 2.26 per million is documented in PubMed. This will be set as approximate rate.
